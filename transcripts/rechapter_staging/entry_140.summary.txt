Louise Broadway was interviewed on March 9, 2013 at Albany State University in Albany, Georgia, by Will Griffin of the Southern Oral History Program with videographer John Bishop, for the Civil Rights History Project of the Library of Congress and the Smithsonian National Museum of African American History and Culture. Born in 1930 in the small town of Newton in Baker County, Georgia, she came up the child of farming parents who owned no land, walking miles to a one-room church school where a single teacher taught the first through seventh grades while white children rode buses past her on the same roads. After graduating in 1948 she wanted college but her family could not afford it, so she worked as a maid for a dollar a day, married Grady Broadway in 1949, and raised five children.

Broadway became a central figure in the Baker County Movement. As one of its secretaries she took notes at the meetings where citizens testified to abuse, records she says now rest at the East Baker Historical Society in Newton. She and her husband enrolled their eldest son in the all-white school and met retaliation, including a threat to call in her sharecropper husband's debt and a beating that hospitalized one of their sons. Working thirty years as assistant to the Albany physician Dr. J. L. Shirley, she watched him treat for free the civil rights workers and citizens beaten by a mob on what she calls Bloody Saturday, after the nearby town of Camilla turned them away, and she recalls her own St. Matthew's Missionary Baptist Church being fired upon as marchers regrouped inside.

Her account moves from these dangers to what the movement won and how she kept serving the community. She marched from a local church to the courthouse to register voters, returning the morning after a county official knocked the organizer Sherrod to the ground, and when she registered she was allowed simply to sign her name. After completing a county emergency-care course at night, she became, by her account, the first Black person in Baker County to ride the ambulance, working without pay so that people of her community could be cared for by one of their own. She closes by naming the movement's gains: freedom of speech where a word to a white person had once brought violence, the courage that desegregated schools gave a new generation, and a doctor's bills quietly forgiven as his contribution to the cause.
