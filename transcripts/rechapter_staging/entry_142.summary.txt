This is a Civil Rights History Project interview with Luis Zapata, conducted by Emilye Crosby of the Southern Oral History Program at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, with John Bishop, on June 27, 2013, at Zapata's home in Silver Spring, Maryland. Zapata describes growing up in Orange County, California, the son of a Mexican American mechanical engineer and an Anglo mother who ran a yarn shop, in a place where his family never discussed racial identity and he learned to fight back after being bullied as a child. He left home young, working gardens, a paper route, and boat hulls before becoming a short-order cook, and a craftsman, ironworker, and carpenter over his life. At San Jose State College he entered as a physics major but grew political amid the era's activism and began organizing with Cesar Chavez's farm workers in the surrounding fields.

When word came that the Mississippi Freedom Labor Union needed help, Zapata volunteered, dropped out of college, and drove a donated car south, beginning roughly four years based mostly in Cleveland in the Delta. He lived in a one-room shack that doubled as the SNCC, COFO, and union office, did voter registration and Head Start work, and organized field strikes that won limited wage gains. He recalls working with strong local women and ministers, among them Fannie Lou Hamer and the union's director, and remembers Amzie Moore, Bob Moses, Stokely Carmichael, and Ivanhoe Donaldson. He drove a delegation of field workers to Washington for the congressional challenge, went north repeatedly to raise money, was jailed many times including at the Jackson fairgrounds, and witnessed debates over nonviolence as the Deacons for Defense visited Cleveland. He describes the deep isolation of the rural Delta, where national news rarely reached him, alongside the singing, dancing, and friendships that sustained the work.

Zapata left Mississippi in the later 1960s when many new volunteers arrived and he no longer felt essential, taking up union work in Washington before years of travel through Mexico, the Caribbean, and Central and South America, where he engaged with traditional Native religion. He returned to the Delta to help elect Mike Espy to Congress by a margin of eighty-two votes, and went on to a long activist life: the Statehood Party in Washington, La Raza, Jesse Jackson's campaigns, a fishing-boat project in revolutionary Grenada, and support work with the Zapatistas in Chiapas. Reflecting in 2013, he names the Supreme Court's recent gutting of the Voting Rights Act and immigration as pressing concerns, argues that organizing and electoral power must work together, and closes by urging younger people to keep fighting for change one step at a time.
