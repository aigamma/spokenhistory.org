Glenda Funchess was interviewed for the Civil Rights History Project at the Hattiesburg Public Library in Hattiesburg, Mississippi, on December 2, 2015, by Emily Crosby. Funchess was born in Hattiesburg on August 17, 1954, which she notes was three months after the Brown v. Board of Education decision, and she grew up the sixth youngest in a family of twelve children. Her mother worked as one of the first homemaker social-worker aides with the Department of Human Services, and her father was a longtime chef at the University of Southern Mississippi.

This excerpt traces Funchess from a segregated childhood, where she became politically aware at age nine during Freedom Summer as SNCC students opened the community's eyes to rights it was not exercising, into the early years of Hattiesburg school desegregation. The bulk of her account is a firsthand description of being recruited by the NAACP education committee, with her sister, to transfer into an affluent white junior high under the district's stair-step, freedom-of-choice plan. She recounts a year of sustained hostility there: nine months of lunchroom isolation, being seated alone, having a history paper on Dr. King destroyed by classmates, the absence of any counseling, racist remarks in choir, and deliberate separation from the only other Black student. The arc closes with her departure to a more welcoming school, her return to the African American Rowan High School, and the city's later zoning, consolidation in 1972, and the founding of a local segregation academy.

This is a partial excerpt of a longer interview. The audio in our holdings covers only roughly the first twenty-three minutes of an interview that, by the Library of Congress reference, runs far longer, so the chapters above reflect only the opening portion of Funchess's full oral history.
