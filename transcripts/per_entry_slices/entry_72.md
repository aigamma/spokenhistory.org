### 72. Junius Williams

**Source**: `transcripts/raw/Junius Williams_interview_20250704_224717/`
**Interviewer / Date**: Joe Mosnier (Whisper-rendered "Joe Manier" — recurring) — Thursday, July 20, 2011, Rutgers University Newark, NJ
**Cameraman**: John Bishop
**Subject**: Junius Williams (b. 1943, Suffolk VA) — son of two music teachers (father was first Black band director in Richmond VA; mother first music coordinator for Northern Virginia schools); grew up Richmond (Barton Avenue); attended Armstrong High School (one of the two canonical Black high schools in Richmond, alongside Maggie Walker HS); high school classmate of Arthur Ashe (who went to Walker). Valedictorian; rejected Northwestern band scholarship (no aid) for Amherst College full ride 1961–65. At Amherst, was president of Students for Racial Equality (SRE), member of Alpha Delta Phi fraternity (their first Black). Spring 1964 worked Crossroads Africa in Kigezi District Uganda (Operation Crossroads Africa, founded by Rev. James H. Robinson). Summer 1964 worked Northern Student Movement Harlem Education Program with Julian T. Houston. February 1965 organized the canonical "Civil Rights Movement: Reform or Revolution?" conference at Mount Holyoke — invited Malcolm X (who couldn't make it due to a France visa issue), Michael Harrington (ice storm), Ossie Davis (came — played all three parts, single-handedly saved the conference). March 1965 drove to Selma; was diverted by Stokely Carmichael's SNCC parallel-Montgomery operation; was arrested 12 March 1965 at corner of Jackson and High Street in Montgomery; spent ~7 days in Kilby State Prison alongside Worth Long. Later attended Yale Law School; later worked Newark NJ housing and civil rights as a lawyer.

#### Notes for Pass 2

- **Mount Holyoke "Reform or Revolution" canonical first-person**: Williams's first-person account of organizing the Feb 12, 1965 Mount Holyoke conference — including the spring-from-Mass-college-students-on-front-page-of-Philadelphia-Daily-News-with-Albany-girls-going-to-jail-singing trigger from May 1963 — and his cold-call to Malcolm X for a $1,000 honorarium nine days before Malcolm's Feb 21 assassination — is canonical first-person testimony on the Northern-campus-side organizing infrastructure that ran parallel to the Southern field work.
- **March on Washington canonical first-person**: Williams's Aug 28, 1963 attendance and the SNCC Freedom Singers' "songs from Danville" striking him profoundly — a song-led recognition that the SNCC sit-in cohort was his generation's vanguard.
- **Selma → Kilby Prison canonical first-person**: Williams's account of being arrested March 12, 1965 at Jackson and High Street in Montgomery, then jailed at Kilby for ~7 days with Worth Long, including Worth Long's "I wouldn't take a blanket unless everyone got a blanket" speech that mobilized the 75-man bullpen overnight — is canonical first-person testimony on Worth Long's organizing in jail, an under-documented event.
- **Sherrod / Virginia Union 1960 sit-in canonical context**: Williams's early-life close-call with the Richmond Virginia Union 1960 sit-ins (he wanted to join but his parents claimed the SNCC kids didn't want HS-age supporters — which Sherrod later confirmed was untrue) — is canonical first-person testimony on Richmond Black-middle-class parental protection patterns.
- **Stokely's Montgomery parallel operation March 1965**: Williams's account of arriving in Montgomery March 1965 expecting to march to Selma, being intercepted by Stokely Carmichael at the Jackson + High Street SNCC office, and learning that SNCC was running a *parallel operation* in Montgomery because it didn't trust SCLC and King to "sell out" on voting rights — is canonical first-person evidence on the under-documented SNCC-vs-SCLC strategic split during the Selma campaign.
