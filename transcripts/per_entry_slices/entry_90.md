**Cameraman**: (not identified)
**Subject**: Mildred Bond Roxborough — born Brownsville, Tennessee (Haywood County); parents organized the first NAACP chapter in Brownsville (one of only 3 TN counties where Blacks could vote at the time). Started selling *The Crisis* magazine at age 9 via direct correspondence with Roy Wilkins (then *Crisis* editor). Attended Howard 1943-45, then NYU. Joined NAACP National Office staff fall 1953 as field secretary; worked under Gloucester Current (Director of Branches). Worked the canonical 1953-54 Baltimore branch under Lillie M. Jackson; the canonical 1954-55 Hoxie AR voluntary desegregation; the canonical 1955 Daisy Bates Little Rock organizing; the canonical 1955-56 Mamie Till Mobley tour. Held ~8-10 positions over decades at NAACP including Director of Operations, Director of Programs. Active full-time staff until 1997.

#### Notes for Pass 2

- **Brownsville TN family NAACP organizing canonical first-person**: Roxborough's first-person account of her parents organizing the first NAACP chapter in Brownsville TN (one of only 3 TN counties where Blacks could vote at the time) + her father's beating + the canonical white-relative-doctor-John-Severe family intervention that prevented his jailing — is canonical first-person testimony on the late-1930s rural Black-NAACP-organizing infrastructure that predated the canonical 1940s mass NAACP expansion.
- **Selling *The Crisis* at age 9 via Roy Wilkins correspondence canonical first-person**: Roxborough's account of writing Roy Wilkins (then *Crisis* editor) for permission to sell *Crisis* subscriptions at age 9 — and receiving a typed signed letter back that authorized her — is canonical first-person testimony on the under-documented child-recruiter pathway by which NAACP cultivated its 1930s-40s second-generation leadership.
- **Clarence Norris Scottsboro pardon canonical first-person**: Roxborough's account of Clarence Norris (last surviving Scottsboro Boy) walking into the NAACP National Office in the late 1960s asking for help clearing his name — and the chain of office decisions leading to his 1976 NY-Governor-Hugh-Carey-mediated AL Governor George Wallace pardon — is canonical first-person testimony on the under-narrated Scottsboro endgame.
- **Mamie Till Bradley/Mobley NAACP tour canonical first-person**: Roxborough's account of arranging Mamie Till Bradley's nationwide NAACP branch tour 1955-56 — and watching Bradley evolve from a grieving mother into "more militant and more informed" speaking demanding civil rights for her son — is canonical first-person testimony on the rarely-told tour-organizing infrastructure that turned the canonical Emmett Till mourning into national pressure.
- **Lillie M. Jackson + Baltimore 1953 baptism canonical first-person**: Roxborough's account of Thurgood Marshall's "Gloster, why are you doing that to that girl?" reaction when Director of Branches Gloster Current assigned the newly-hired-1953 Roxborough to do a Baltimore membership campaign under Lillie M. Jackson is canonical first-person Marshall-as-mentor anecdote.
- **Hoxie AR 1955 voluntary desegregation canonical first-person**: Roxborough's account of being assigned by Thurgood Marshall to Hoxie AR in 1955 — the first voluntary post-*Brown* school-board desegregation in a former Confederate state — is canonical first-person testimony on a less-foregrounded *Brown*-implementation event.
- **NAACP women in leadership canonical first-person**: Roxborough's enumeration of NAACP women in key roles (Daisy Lampkin, Juanita Jackson Mitchell, Daisy Bates, Ruby Hurley, June Shagaloff, Lucille Black, Bobbie Branch, Constance Baker Motley, Septima Clark, Ella Baker, Eleanor Roosevelt, Frances Hooks, Margaret Bush Wilson, Roslyn Brock) is canonical first-person testimony on women's central but often-erased role in NAACP organizing history.
