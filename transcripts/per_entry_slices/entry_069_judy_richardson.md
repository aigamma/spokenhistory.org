**Interviewer / Date**: Emilye Crosby — December 9, 2015, Judy Richardson's home in Silver Spring, MD (outside DC)
**Cameraman**: John Bishop and Guha Shankar (Whisper-rendered "Lou Haas-Shanker"; Library of Congress folklife specialist)
**Subject**: Judy Richardson — born March 10, 1944, Tarrytown, NY (Westchester County). Daughter of William King Richardson (UAW Local treasurer at the Tarrytown Chevrolet/Fisher Body plant; died of a heart attack on the assembly line when Judy was seven) and Mellie Louise Richardson née Tucker (seamstress, jazz pianist, numbers-runner / bookmaker after husband's death). Older sister Carita "Kita" Richardson, nine years older, attended Bennington; later organized Harry Belafonte SNCC fundraising concerts on Long Island summer 1964. Judy attended Swarthmore College 1962 (one of eight Black freshmen, all housed with Quaker classmates). Joined SPAC (Swarthmore Political Action Committee, SDS chapter — co-chairs Mimi Feingold and Carl Wittman) freshman fall 1962. Took the bus to Cambridge, MD weekend SNCC support (CNAC — Cambridge Non-Violent Action Committee — Gloria Richardson's organization, no relation) starting fall 1962. Took fall 1963 semester off Swarthmore to work full-time SNCC Cambridge with Reggie Robinson. November 1963 transitioned to SNCC National Office in Atlanta (8 1/2 Raymond Street) as James Forman's personal secretary (could type 90 wpm, take regular shorthand — both learned in Tarrytown junior high's "secretarial track" that the school routed her into because she was Black despite being top of her class). Worked in the National Office through the Kennedy assassination Nov 22, 1963 mass meeting. Later worked on *Eyes on the Prize* (Blackside productions, 1980s).

#### Notes for Pass 2

- **SNCC National Office Raymond Street canonical first-person**: Richardson's first-person account of walking into 8 1/2 Raymond Street SNCC HQ in November 1963 and meeting Forman sweeping the stairs in overalls — and Forman's pedagogical "no job is too lowly" practice — is canonical first-person testimony on the SNCC ethic that contrasts sharply with the better-known NAACP / SCLC hierarchical organizational cultures.
- **Forman secretarial work canonical first-person**: Richardson's account of being routed into the Tarrytown junior high "secretarial track" in 7th grade specifically because she was Black despite topping her class — and the irony that the 90-wpm typing and shorthand skills she learned there became her qualification for serving as Forman's personal secretary in the SNCC National Office through the Kennedy assassination mass meeting — is canonical first-person testimony on the unintended-consequence dynamics of 1950s Northern de facto segregation.
- **Kennedy assassination SNCC reaction canonical first-person**: Richardson's account of the November 1963 SNCC staff meeting after JFK was killed — Dinky Romilly crying, Forman counseling strategic caution given that "the federal government hasn't done deadly squat for us" — is canonical first-person testimony on the SNCC orientation toward Kennedy, who many in the broader Black community mourned more uncomplicatedly.
- **Numbers-running / UAW pension household economics canonical first-person**: Richardson's first-person account of her mother's economic survival as a UAW Local widow + a numbers-runner who booked bets for the Italian, Polish, Irish, and Black families of under-the-hill Tarrytown — and the moment her mother won the numbers and bought fur coats with her sister Helen — is canonical first-person testimony on the under-documented working-class Northern Black numbers-economy that paralleled the better-known Bronzeville / Harlem / Pittsburgh Hill District policy-shop networks.
- **Cambridge MD weekend bus organizing canonical first-person**: Richardson's account of the weekly Saturday-bus-down-to-Cambridge-MD pattern from Swarthmore campus — and the inter-college coalition (Swarthmore, Morgan State, Gallaudet, NC State) that converged on Cambridge MD weekends 1962–63 — is canonical first-person testimony on the Cambridge MD movement's regional Northeast college-student support network that intersected with Gloria Richardson's CNAC organizing.
- **Birth of a Nation canonical Hollywood-vs-history**: Richardson's report of her mother's 1915 *Birth of a Nation* viewing trauma — and her own 1957-period Sleepy Hollow AP-history-class trauma when the same *Birth of a Nation* iconography appeared in her Reconstruction-section textbook illustration with white classmates looking back at her — is canonical first-person testimony on a multi-generational pedagogical-trauma pattern that *Eyes on the Prize* later worked against.
