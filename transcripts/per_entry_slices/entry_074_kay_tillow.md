**Interviewer / Date**: David Cline (Whisper-rendered "David Klein" — recurring) — August 14, 2013, Louisville, KY
**Cameraman**: John Bishop, Media Generation / UCLA
**Subject**: Kay Tillow (b. 1942 Paducah, KY; raised Metropolis, IL) — daughter of a Southern Illinois Teachers College alumna teacher and a furniture-store-owning farmer. Activated as a HS student by Anne Braden's *The Wall Between* (gifted by her Presbyterian minister Maynard Eltzman). Attended University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign 1960–64 (intermittent); first picket line at U of I NAACP for store hiring discrimination ("In the Land of Lincoln, Discrimination is Stinkin'"). Spring 1962 saw Cairo, IL SNCC demonstrations including Mary McCollum's brutal knife wound. Summer 1962 study abroad at University of Ghana under Conor Cruise O'Brien (saw W.E.B. Du Bois receive emeritus degree alongside Shirley Graham Du Bois). Returned August 1963 (Du Bois had died Aug 27; March on Washington Aug 28); rejoined U of I NAACP. December 1963 NAACP-chapter delegation to Atlanta SNCC HQ met Prathia Hall, James Forman; went to Hattiesburg for voter-registration demonstrations (Bob Moses in jail with a badly-beaten white movement worker — pre-Freedom Summer); decision to drop out of school. Worked Appalachian Committee for Full Employment Eastern Kentucky 1964; met Walter Tillow at Highlander; joined March 5, 1964 March on Frankfort for Jobs and Freedom. After 1966, UE (United Electrical Workers) organizer (Northern PA); 1969 onward Local 1199 hospital workers union organizer (Pittsburgh, PA): won Jewish Home and Hospital for the Aged Pittsburgh Christmas Eve 1969 sit-in (with Henry Nicholas). Later Pennsylvania state collective-bargaining-rights expansion to hospital workers (1970), pre-dating the 1975 NLRB amendments. Carried on into single-payer healthcare organizing (HR 676, Rep. John Conyers's Expanded and Improved Medicare for All Act). Currently working on the All Unions Committee for Single Payer Healthcare HR 676 (605 union endorsements as of 2013, 43 state AFL-CIO federations, 146 Central Labor Councils).

#### Notes for Pass 2

- **Economic-justice / labor canonical first-person**: Tillow's first-person account that "public accommodations was not winning equality and public accommodations would not end the inequality because there was an economic base to it" — and her decision in 1964 to leave the University of Illinois mid-degree (graduating eventually 1967) to work the Appalachian Committee for Full Employment in Eastern Kentucky — is canonical first-person testimony on the under-documented economic-justice wing of the Civil Rights Movement that the SCLC's later Poor People's Campaign 1968 attempted to centralize.
- **1199 hospital workers union in PA canonical first-person**: Tillow's account of the Dec 24, 1969 Jewish Home and Hospital for the Aged Pittsburgh sit-in — where the Rabbi from Rodef Shalom synagogue oversaw the eventual election — was the canonical first union win in Pennsylvania for hospital workers, predating the 1970 PA state collective-bargaining law and the 1975 NLRB amendment that finally brought hospital workers under federal labor law.
- **Anne Braden / SCEF canonical first-person**: Tillow's first-person account of being radicalized by Anne Braden's book *The Wall Between* (1958) as a high school student in Metropolis IL — gifted by her Presbyterian minister Maynard Eltzman — is canonical first-person testimony on Braden's outsized influence on white Southern (and border-state) youth in the late 1950s, before SNCC existed.
- **HR 676 (Conyers single-payer) canonical first-person**: Tillow's first-person account of organizing the All Unions Committee for Single Payer Healthcare HR 676 — securing 605 union endorsements + 43 state AFL-CIO federations + 146 Central Labor Councils + 22 international unions — is canonical first-person testimony on the labor wing of US single-payer healthcare organizing, which the Affordable Care Act process explicitly bypassed.

The project was partially completed by the Du Bois Centre in Accra after his death and produced its first volume in 1997. Canonical Pan-African intellectual project of the early 1960s.
