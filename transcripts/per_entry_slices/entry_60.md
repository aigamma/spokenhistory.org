#### Notes for Pass 2

- **Partial read**: ~76% of the 95 KB transcript was read. Pass 2 must read the remaining 24% covering Mulholland's post-Tougaloo 1964-65 movement work, her marriage and family life, and her contemporary documentary-filmmaker / civil-rights-educator second career.
- **Foundational white-Southern-female-Movement canonical first-person**: Mulholland's transcript is one of the most-detailed first-person sources in the corpus on the foundational paradox of why "white Southerners — not Northerners — were the first white people to seek out the young Black activists in SNCC and CORE." Her framing that white Southerners were working "at home" on what they saw as their own problems while "Yankees up there were looking down their nose at us" is canonical first-person material on the often-overlooked regional dynamics of the white-civil-rights-activist cohort.
- **The Parchman Farm Rabbi Nussbaum prayer-meeting canonical first-person**: Mulholland's account of Rabbi Perry Nussbaum's weekly drive up from Jackson to Parchman Farm — and his prayer-meetings during which he would slip in world-news in English between Hebrew prayer to evade the listening guards — is canonical first-person material on this often-undocumented institutional support for the imprisoned 1961 Freedom Riders.
- **Women Power Unlimited canonical first-person**: Mulholland's account of Clarie Collins Harvey's foundational 1961 Black-churchwomen-organized cell-network that delivered toothbrushes, shower shoes, postage stamps, and feminine products into the Jackson city jail and Hinds County jail for the Freedom Riders is canonical first-person material. Pass 2 should add Clarie Collins Harvey and Women Power Unlimited to the ground-truth corpus.
- **May 28, 1963 Jackson Woolworth's sit-in canonical first-person**: Mulholland's first-person account of how she ended up sitting at the Woolworth's lunch counter as a "spotter" who joined the besieged sit-in mid-attack is canonical first-person material that complements Anne Moody's *Coming of Age in Mississippi* (1968) chapter on the same event. The canonical mid-attack photograph image of Moody / Norman / Lewis / Mulholland under the mustard / sugar / ketchup deluge is one of the most-iconic 1960s Civil Rights Movement images.
- **First white Tougaloo student canonical first-person**: Mulholland's account of her Fall 1961 enrollment as the first white student to enroll full-time at Tougaloo — including the first-night dorm encounter when a fellow-resident screamed thinking Mulholland was a ghost — is canonical first-person material on the foundational Tougaloo integration.
- **"Yankees do not know how to sing or clap" canonical first-person**: Mulholland's account of the Hinds County Jail and Parchman Farm 1961 Freedom Rider singing-as-resistance — and her judgment that "those Yankees didn't know how to sing... their clapping was way off... we had spiritual singing in the South" — is canonical first-person material on the foundational cultural-difference dynamics inside the integrated 1961 Freedom Rider cohort.
