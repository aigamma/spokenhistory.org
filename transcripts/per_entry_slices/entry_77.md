### 77. Leesco Guster

**Source**: `transcripts/raw/Leesco Guster_interview_20250704_230807/`
**Interviewer / Date**: Emilye Crosby (Whisper-rendered "Emily Kausby") — December 3, 2015, Port Gibson, MS
**Cameraman**: John Bishop and Guha Shankar (Whisper-rendered "Gouha Shanker" — recurring)
**Subject**: Leesco Guster (b. March 26, 1936, Port Gibson, Mississippi) — born across the street from the Port Gibson Health Department to a single mother who later moved to Vicksburg ("Big Sput" — Whisper). Returned to Port Gibson 1953; worked domestic jobs (lived in Scofield-North household), Montgomery Ward in Chicago briefly. Married Harry Guster ~1959 (24 years her senior; canonical Claiborne County / Louisiana lumber contractor). Activated by Charles Evers + Rudy Shields voter-registration drive 1965-66; her car was the canonical SW Mississippi vehicle that hauled people to the polls. Hosted Tucki Tate (African civil rights worker; uncertain spelling), Lily Mae Williams, multiple civil rights lawyers in her home. Volunteered for NAACP voter registration despite not being an NAACP member initially — her purpose was "voting, a dollar, recognition." Was a named co-defendant in the canonical *NAACP v. Claiborne Hardware* (1982) Supreme Court case (where Port Gibson white merchants sued the local NAACP for 1969-70 boycott economic damages; SCOTUS unanimously ruled the boycott protected by First Amendment). Lost her domestic-work job at Hillary's home (the daughter of Mr. "Walk-Cut" / Walcott from Mansfield, Ohio) when Hillary discovered her voter-registration canvassing work.

**Cross-references**:
- Rudy Shields (canonical SW Mississippi NAACP organizer; recurring across Port Gibson, Yazoo, Hattiesburg corpus; brave but sickly; lived in Yazoo MS later, died ~late 1970s).
- Charles Evers (canonical Medgar's brother; NAACP MS field director; came to Port Gibson with Rudy Shields 1965-66).
- *NAACP v. Claiborne Hardware* (458 U.S. 886, 1982) — canonical First Amendment-protected boycott case; subject of decades-long litigation that Guster herself was named in.
- Nate "Dusty" Jackson — canonical Port Gibson civil rights worker killed (uncertain date).
- Hudson family — *not* the same Hudson Salvage Hattiesburg family (cross-ref Ladners #75 same set); this is a different Hudson in Port Gibson (white merchant who later supported boycott terms).
- Bobby Gage — local lawyer who claimed Gusters were behind on house payments; the rumor was disproved when Guster produced receipts.
- Kenneth Ross — Port Gibson NAACP president (recent); organized the civil rights mural.
- Reverend Dawson — Port Gibson NAACP president (predecessor) who stepped down.

#### Notes for Pass 2

- **Port Gibson boycott canonical first-person**: Guster's first-person account of the 1969-71 Port Gibson NAACP boycott — the canonical *NAACP v. Claiborne Hardware* (1982 SCOTUS) case — is canonical first-person testimony from one of the named co-defendants. Her critique of the second boycott extending to merchants who had already met the original demands ("Cleveland and Hardware, P.G. Wigley and folks who had not done [what we asked]") is canonical first-person dissent on a boycott tactic that is otherwise lionized as a uniform Black community action.
- **Hillary firing canonical first-person**: Guster's first-person account of overhearing her white employer Hillary (from Mansfield, Ohio) using racial slurs about civil rights work — and being fired the same day — is canonical first-person testimony on the economic-retaliation pattern in SW Mississippi 1965-66.
- **Voter-registration-canvassing-car canonical first-person**: Guster's role as the Port Gibson "movement car" — driving Rudy Shields and others around the county to register voters — and the rumor that she was being paid which forced her to call out donations at mass meetings to clear her name — is canonical first-person testimony on the local logistics of the voter-registration push.
- **Rocky Springs canonical first-person**: Guster's account of being threatened by a white landowner with a shotgun on his pickup truck at Rocky Springs, then chased by the same truck across a one-lane bridge — and Rudy Shields in her back seat with both shotgun and pistol — is canonical first-person testimony on the immediate physical danger of door-to-door canvassing in rural Claiborne County.
- **KKK threat phone call canonical first-person**: Guster's account of receiving a KKK "are you tired of living?" phone call before a planned banquet — and the deacons (Deacons for Defense?) stationing armed guards on her property — is canonical first-person testimony on the convergence of armed self-defense and movement protection in Port Gibson.
- **James Miller / Carolyn Miller cross-corpus**: Guster mentions James Miller as one of the young people in her youth choir cohort. This cross-references transcript #12 (Carolyn Miller and James Miller, Port Gibson Movement).
