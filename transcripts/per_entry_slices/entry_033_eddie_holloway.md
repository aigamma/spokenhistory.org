### 33. Eddie Holloway

**Source**: `transcripts/raw/Eddie Holloway_interview_20250704_195135/`

**Interviewer / Date**: Emily Crosby (Southern Oral History Program at UNC Chapel Hill); videographer John Bishop and Bhu Hashankar — 2 December 2015, at the University of Southern Mississippi (USM), Hattiesburg
**Subject**: Eddie Holloway — Hattiesburg-born (1952) USM Dean of Students; raised on Mobile Street (the segregation-era Black commercial district), son of beautician Beatrice "Bee" Gibson Holloway (died when he was 11) and 60-year fertilizer-company laborer Willie Holloway; spent summers in Bessemer, Alabama with civil-rights-conscious elder aunts/uncles Ears and Lewis; attended W.H. Jones Elementary and Rowan High School in Hattiesburg under principal N.R. Burger; enrolled at USM in 1970 (five years after the first Black USM students Elaine Armstrong and Gwendolyn Branch); first-ever Black USM resident assistant and Elam Arms residence-hall staff member; now serves on the Mississippi Civil Rights Commission and the Mississippi Coalition Against Sexual Assault; reflects across the interview on the delicate "negotiating posture" of Black Hattiesburg educators like Burger, the Theron Lynd voting-registrar fight, Vernon Dahmer's 1966 assassination, the Clyde Kennard tragedy, and his own framework of "bridge-building" over confrontation.
**Cross-references**: Holloway↔Magee (#21; both Hattiesburg movement figures, both attended Rowan High and were taught by Clarence McGee); Holloway↔Vernon Dahmer family (the canonical Hattiesburg NAACP/voting-rights martyr referenced throughout); Holloway↔William Carey College / Dr. J. Ralph Noonkester (1965 Mississippi private-college desegregation context).
