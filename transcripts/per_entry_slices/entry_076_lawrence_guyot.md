**Subject**: Lawrence Guyot (b. 1939 Pass Christian, MS; d. 2012) — born and raised in Pass Christian MS, Gulf Coast (a community uniquely integrated for Mississippi due to heavy Catholic Church + labor union + Keesler AFB influences). Attended Tougaloo College 1959–63 (Ed King, Ernst Borinski, John Salter Jr., A.A. Branch). Joined SNCC 1962; key figure in COFO 1962 Clarksdale founding; canonical MFDP chair 1964 onward. Was beaten almost to death in Winona MS jail June 9, 1963 (the canonical incident in which Fannie Lou Hamer, Annelle Ponder, June Johnson, Euvester Simpson were also beaten). Worked Greenwood, Hattiesburg, Forrest County (Theron Lynd registrar case). Chaired MFDP through 1968 (with merger with the Loyalist Democrats at Chicago Convention 1968). Moved to DC 1968; became ANC Commissioner, DC city council activist, Adrian Fenty supporter. Husband of Monica Klein Guyot (white woman SNCC volunteer from California). Died Nov 2012.

#### Notes for Pass 2

- **Pass Christian / Catholic-and-labor influence canonical first-person**: Guyot's first-person account of growing up in Pass Christian MS — uniquely integrated for Mississippi due to the heavy Catholic Church desegregation order 1957/58 ("excommunication or integration, make your choice by Friday"), the Ingalls Shipyard / Keesler AFB labor union and federal-employment influence, and Bidwell Adams's protection of him as a teenage Black slot-machine operator — is canonical first-person testimony on the historically-distinctive Gulf Coast Mississippi exception to the deep-Mississippi-segregation pattern.
- **Winona June 1963 jail beating canonical first-person**: Guyot's account of being beaten by 12 people in Winona jail, threatened with having his testicles burned with wooden sticks, fighting to stay conscious knowing unconsciousness = death — and being saved by Roberta Galler's distress calls + the Medgar Evers assassination news galvanizing federal pressure + Andy Young, Bevel, Frank Smith intervention — is canonical first-person testimony on one of the most dangerous Civil Rights Movement events. Cross-corpus with Ladners #75 (Dorie was in Jackson when the calls came through).
- **MFDP Atlantic City 1964 canonical first-person**: Guyot's first-person account of chairing the Mississippi Freedom Democratic Party in absentia (he was in Mississippi jail) during the August 1964 Atlantic City Democratic Convention challenge — and his post-hoc judgment that "I'm proud of the fact that I'm the chairman of the Mississippi Freedom Democratic Party" who led the Convention into "what I consider the most courageous, pragmatic political decision in American politics" by rejecting LBJ/Humphrey's two-seat compromise offer — is canonical first-person testimony from the canonical MFDP leader.
- **Voting Rights Act of 1965 Section 5 canonical first-person**: Guyot's first-person account that "*Allen v. State Board of Elections* (1969) was my idea" — applying Section 5 preclearance to private litigants, not just the Attorney General — is canonical first-person testimony on a key behind-the-scenes Voting Rights Act expansion. The Supreme Court ruled 8-1 in MFDP's favor; the Justice Department filed amicus. Guyot cites Anthony Lewis's *Gideon's Trumpet* (1964) describing LBJ as a vote-stealer as inspiration: "if you had the choice between trained by being given guidance by a thief or by a bishop, choose the thief."
- **Hartman Turnbow / Holmes County canonical first-person**: Guyot's account of going to the Holmes County Sheriff with a release-by-bond form he himself had signed for Turnbow's release from a Leflore County murder charge — and the Department of Justice's rigorous defense of Turnbow on the basis that the charges were retaliatory — is canonical first-person testimony on the canonical Holmes County 1963 Hartman Turnbow case (his house firebombed, he fired back killing one attacker, then was charged with arson — Department of Justice defended him).
- **Same-sex marriage / DC City Council canonical first-person**: Guyot's first-person account of his late-career support for same-sex marriage in DC — and his refusal to separate his religious beliefs from his political action — is canonical first-person testimony on the post-1990s evolution of canonical Civil Rights Movement leaders on LGBTQ+ rights. He cites his interracial marriage to his white wife Monica as the personal foundation of his analogizing.
