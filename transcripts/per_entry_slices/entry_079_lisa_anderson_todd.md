### 79. Lisa Anderson Todd

**Source**: `transcripts/raw/Lisa Anderson Todd_interview_20250704_231814/`

**Cameraman**: John Bishop
**Subject**: Lisa Anderson Todd (b. ~1942 NYC, raised Summit, NJ) — daughter of corporate lawyer Carl Anderson (Republican; Hibbing, MN-raised; later UCC + United Negro College Fund national giving chairman) and a psychiatric social worker mother. Attended Cornell University 1960-64 (American Studies + Government, Kappa Alpha Theta sorority). Summer 1962 Experiment in International Living in Northern Sweden. Summer 1963 two work camps: (a) Ecumenical Voluntary Service of the World Council of Churches project at Tougaloo College Mississippi (met Bob Moses, traveled to Greenwood SNCC office + Williams Chapel Ruleville for canonical Fannie Lou Hamer dinner Aug 1, 1963 where Hamer recounted the canonical Winona beating including "I didn't know that flesh could be so hard"); (b) American Friends Service Committee voter-registration project Greensboro NC (registered 803 people). Volunteered for SNCC Freedom Summer 1964 in Greenville MS (Washington County), working voter registration + Mississippi Freedom Democratic Party precinct organizing. Drove to Atlantic City 1964 with Pat Vail. After Stanford Law School, became a DC-area civil rights / public-interest lawyer. Has been writing a memoir about the canonical 1964 MFDP Atlantic City Convention challenge for 7+ years (since 2006 retirement), with extensive research at the LBJ tapes archive + Anne Romaine oral history collection.

#### Notes for Pass 2

- **Atlantic City Convention 1964 canonical first-person**: Anderson Todd's first-person eyewitness account of the Aug 25, 1964 MFDP delegates being moved from the main church room to a smaller room to decide on the LBJ/Humphrey two-seat-compromise offer — and Martin Luther King Jr. being "hushed out" before he could speak in favor of the compromise — is canonical first-person testimony from one of the few white volunteers who was at the church for the entire deliberation. Her 7+ years of LBJ-tapes research has expanded her interpretation: she argues that LBJ opposed seating MFDP not just out of fear of losing southern electoral votes, but out of fear of losing the entire election to Goldwater on a racial-backlash basis (citing the Rochester + Harlem 1964 riots) — a less-commonly-cited interpretive frame.
- **Aug 1, 1963 Hamer dinner canonical first-person**: Anderson Todd's first-person account of dinner with Fannie Lou Hamer at her Ruleville home Aug 1, 1963 — including Hamer's first telling of the Winona beating story ("I didn't know that flesh could be so hard") to outside white visitors — predates the canonical Atlantic City Aug 22, 1964 nationally-televised account by more than a year. This is one of the earliest documented Hamer-tells-Winona-beating moments.
- **Hodding Carter pool July 1964 canonical first-person**: Anderson Todd's account of the entire 35-person Greenville SNCC project being invited to swim in Hodding Carter's pool (with Dorothy Jones the housekeeper as the canonical local mediator) — and being driven off by neighbors on horseback who thought something illegal was happening on the property — is canonical first-person testimony on a recurring story across multiple Greenville-volunteer memoirs (also cross-references Charlie Cobb's Southern Mississippi Univ. oral history).
- **Walter Tillow + SNCC fall-1963 recruitment canonical first-person**: Anderson Todd received the canonical fall-1963 letter from Walter Tillow recruiting her for Freedom Summer — making her an early applicant + cross-corpus reference to Kay Tillow #74 same iteration (Walter's wife).
- **Anne Romaine oral history collection canonical citation**: Anderson Todd cites the Anne Romaine collection at UNC (recorded 1966-67) as the foundational corpus for her own research on MFDP. Romaine's collection is canonical SNCC oral history.
- **Greensboro NC voter-registration summer 1963 canonical first-person**: Anderson Todd registered 803 voters in the Greensboro Black community summer 1963 with AFSC — the canonical late-summer 1963 voter-registration push that built on the canonical Feb 1, 1960 Greensboro 4 sit-ins.
