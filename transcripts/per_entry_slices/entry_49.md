### 49. Gloria Hayes Richardson (Dandridge)

**Source**: `transcripts/raw/Gloria Hayes Richardson_interview_20250704_205935/`
**Interviewer / Date**: Joe Mosnier (Southern Oral History Program at UNC Chapel Hill); videographer John Bishop — at Richardson's Manhattan apartment in the Flatiron District (date not stated explicitly; likely 2011-2012 era based on the CRHP chronology; her *Manning Marable's Malcolm X* discussion suggests 2011-2012)
**Subject**: Gloria Hayes Richardson Dandridge (1922–2021) — born Baltimore, raised Cambridge MD; Howard University 1938-42 (E. Franklin Frazier sociology student, Rayford Logan history student); foundational woman leader of the Cambridge Movement (1962-64), the most-significant non-Southern Black-community-led movement of the era and Maryland's longest peacetime National Guard occupation (July 1963-July 1964); founder/chair of the Cambridge Nonviolent Action Committee (CNAC) — the adult-led local-movement organization that grew out of the early 1961-62 SNCC-led teen organizing in Cambridge; co-signer with Robert F. Kennedy and General George M. Gelston of the foundational "Treaty of Cambridge" July 23, 1963 (the first federally-mediated Black-community / municipal-government desegregation agreement); the only woman invited (then sidelined backstage) to the August 28, 1963 March on Washington; close personal ally and stage-companion of Malcolm X 1964-65 — the canonical photograph of the two of them on the stage at the Cambridge mass meetings is a foundational Movement image, and Richardson was scheduled to be at the Audubon Ballroom the day Malcolm was assassinated February 21, 1965; moved to NYC 1965 with her second husband Frank Dandridge; reflective on the post-1965 deflection-of-leadership pressures and her independent stance against the NAACP/SCLC/CORE national leadership's prescriptions for Cambridge.
**Cross-references**: Richardson↔Robert F. Kennedy (the canonical July 23, 1963 Treaty of Cambridge negotiator); Richardson↔General George M. Gelston (Maryland National Guard commander whose 1963-64 Cambridge occupation Richardson trusted as "the only fair-minded white official we encountered"); Richardson↔Malcolm X (Richardson and Malcolm X appeared together at multiple 1964 Detroit / Cambridge / NYC events; Richardson's mother offered Malcolm's family their NJ parsonage's third floor as a safe-house in the months after the first Shabazz-home bombing); Richardson↔E. Franklin Frazier (Howard sociology professor — same as Thelwell #34.20); Richardson↔Reverend Albert Cleage / Reverend C.L. Franklin (Detroit Black-nationalist 1963 Walk-to-Freedom and Group on Advanced Leadership figures); Richardson↔Burke Marshall (the JFK-RFK Justice Department Civil Rights Division head 1961-65); Richardson↔W. Robert Ming Jr. (the NAACP / University of Chicago attorney; same as Bassett #38.18); Richardson↔Frederick St. Clair (Richardson's cousin and Cambridge-movement co-leader); Richardson↔Donna Richardson (Gloria's daughter, age 14-16 during the Cambridge movement, one of the organizers of the Cambridge high-school student protests).

#### Notes for Pass 2

- **Full read**: This transcript was fully read end-to-end.
- **Cambridge Movement canonical first-person**: Richardson is the foundational figure in the Cambridge Movement (1962-64), one of the most-significant non-Southern Black-community-led movements of the era and the site of Maryland's longest peacetime National Guard occupation. Pair with any other Cambridge-Movement transcripts (Frederick St. Clair / Donna Richardson / Bill Hansen / Reginald Robinson) and with the foundational Annette Brock historiography.
- **"Treaty of Cambridge" July 23, 1963 canonical first-person**: Richardson's account of the foundational federally-mediated Black-community / municipal-government desegregation agreement is canonical first-person material. Pair with Robert F. Kennedy biographies and Burke Marshall papers.
- **Richardson's "the only woman invited then sidelined" canonical March on Washington first-person**: Richardson's account of being brought to the March on Washington stage by Bayard Rustin only to find her chair removed, of being put in a taxi back to her hotel before Martin Luther King Jr. spoke, alongside Lena Horne (who had brought Rosa Parks to the media), is canonical first-person material on the gender dynamics of the foundational August 1963 March. Pair with Daisy Bates / Anna Hedgeman / Dorothy Height transcripts on the same theme.
- **Richardson↔Malcolm X canonical first-person**: Richardson's first meeting with Malcolm at the June 1963 Detroit Walk to Freedom (held at Reverend Albert Cleage's church, parallel to but distinct from the Reverend C.L. Franklin SCLC event), her mother's offer of NJ parsonage third-floor safe-house for Malcolm's family after the Shabazz-home firebombing, the Nation of Islam Baltimore-emissary warning to "stay off the stage with Malcolm X," and Richardson's scheduled-but-not-attended February 21, 1965 Audubon Ballroom presence — all are canonical first-person material on Richardson's foundational Malcolm-X solidarity relationship.
- **General Gelston as "fair-minded white military commander" canonical first-person**: Richardson's account of General George M. Gelston's professionalism during the July 1963 – July 1964 Cambridge National Guard occupation — his use of his second-in-command to bypass the Fort Belvoir wiretap, his withdrawal from City Council meetings when challenged, his off-duty soldiers being given lemonade by Black ladies on guard-corner duty — is canonical first-person material on the rare cases where National Guard occupations supported rather than suppressed Black-community organizing.
- **Phillips Packing Company union organizing canonical first-person**: Richardson's account of the interracial union-organizing meetings at the "black rod and gun club" during the Cambridge occupation — where ILGWU/garment-workers-union organizers from New York met with Black and white Phillips employees who were too afraid to meet at the American Legion or similar venues — is canonical first-person material on rarely-documented labor-civil-rights coalition work.
- **Quillenwood Cray → Claude McKay / *If We Must Die* canonical recital first-person**: Richardson's recital of McKay's foundational 1919 Harlem Renaissance poem "If We Must Die" as her personal-grounding text under the strain of leadership pressures is canonical first-person on the Harlem-Renaissance literary heritage's role in 1960s Black-Power-era movement-leader self-formation.
- **The "Cambridge was just the women's room" thesis correction canonical first-person**: Richardson explicitly corrects the historiographical assumption that the Cambridge movement was "just the women's room" — emphasizing that women worked during the day because they were available while men worked and that men guarded the community at night including with armed defense; this canonical first-person source-material complicates simple gendered framings of the Cambridge movement.
