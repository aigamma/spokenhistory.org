**Interviewer / Date**: Joe Mosnier, Southern Oral History Program at UNC Chapel Hill — undated 2011, home of Mr. Richard and Mrs. Anne Sobol, New Orleans LA
**Cameraman**: John Bishop; with Elaine Nichols (Smithsonian curator) also present
**Subject**: Richard Barry Sobol (b. NYC, raised on West Side of Manhattan; father a lawyer + mother a NYC mathematics teacher; Bronx HS of Science → Union College → Columbia Law School → clerkship for Judge Paul Hays on 2nd Circuit → FTC commissioner's assistant → spring 1963 joined Arnold Fortas & Porter as associate). Volunteered with Lawyers Constitutional Defense Committee (LCDC) summer 1964 but was rejected (LCDC only sent lawyers admitted to the bar in southern states); accepted summer 1965 assignment in New Orleans; spent 3 weeks there with such immediate impact (school-desegregation reversal in West Feliciana Parish against Judge E. Gordon West within 8 days of arrival; multi-front Bogalusa work) that he convinced LCDC director Henry Schwarzschild to create a permanent New Orleans staff position. Joined LCDC New Orleans full-time July 1966; worked under and in collaboration with Black civil-rights law firm Collins, Douglas, and Elie (Robert Collins → first Black federal judge in the South; Nils Douglas; Lolis Elie). Filed the canonical Crown Zellerbach Title VII case in Bogalusa LA — including the legendary George Cooper trunk-of-books cross-examination of the Wonderlic-test expert witnesses before Judge Frederick J.R. Heebe in summer 1967; co-wrote the canonical 1969 Harvard Law Review article with Cooper on Title VII seniority systems and lines-of-progression remedies (most-cited Law Review article of its era; influential through Griggs v. Duke Power 1971 + corpus of Title VII jurisprudence until U.S. v. Teamsters 1976 walked back its core holding). Arrested by Leander Perez (Plaquemines Parish DA, canonical segregationist) on the trumped-up charge of "practicing law without a license" while filing the appeal for Black Plaquemines client Gary Duncan; the resulting Sobol v. Perez federal civil-rights-attorney-protection injunction (LSU+LDOJ-intervened 3-judge district court ruling) became canonical protection for outside civil rights attorneys in southern state courts. Co-counsel with James Nabrit III (Howard University) on Duncan v. Louisiana — the canonical 1968 Supreme Court ruling that the Sixth Amendment right to jury trial applies to state criminal proceedings via the Fourteenth Amendment.
