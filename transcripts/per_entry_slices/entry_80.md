### 80. Lonnie C. King

**Source**: `transcripts/raw/Lonnie C. King_interview_20250704_232613/`
**Interviewer / Date**: Emilye Crosby (Whisper-rendered "Emily Klausby") — May 29, 2013, Fulton County (Southwest) Library, Atlanta GA
**Cameraman**: John Bishop
**Subject**: Lonnie C. King (b. Arlington, GA — Calhoun County; raised by maternal grandfather Rev. Joseph P. Smith until age 8, then moved to Atlanta with his mother Bertha Lee Smith King) — canonical Atlanta Student Movement founder; chaired the Committee on the Appeal for Human Rights (COAHR) from Feb 1960 onward. Attended Morehouse College 1957-60 (first-string fullback football scholarship). Recruited Joseph Pierce and Julian Bond as the two other founders Feb 1, 1960 (Greensboro day) at Yates and Milton Drugs. Drafted by the AU Center presidents (Rufus Clement, Benjamin E. Mays, Albert Manley, James P. Brawley, Harry V. Richardson, Frank Cunningham) to produce a manifesto — "An Appeal for Human Rights" published March 9, 1960 (principally written by Roslyn Pope, with Julian Bond + Charles Black). Led the May 17, 1960 mass march to the GA Capitol on Brown decision anniversary. Co-founded the *Atlanta Inquirer* July 4, 1960 with Kassuth Hill + William Strong. Was acid-attacked by a white assailant outside Manns Brothers grocery in West End Atlanta during the 1960 boycott. Was jailed with MLK Jr. in Oct 1960 Fulton County jail (the canonical Atlanta sit-in arrest that triggered the JFK call to Coretta Scott King + Robert Kennedy's call to Judge Mitchell → 1960 election turning point).

#### Notes for Pass 2

- **Atlanta Student Movement Feb 1960 founding canonical first-person**: Lonnie King's first-person account of the Feb 1, 1960 (Greensboro day) Yates and Milton Drugs newspaper-reading moment when he approached Joseph Pierce and then Julian Bond about organizing — is canonical first-person testimony from the founding-trio leader who has been less foregrounded in mainstream Movement narrative than the Greensboro Four.
- **AU Center presidents March 1960 meeting canonical first-person**: King's account of being summoned to the Harkness Hall conference room by Mrs. Hill (Mays's secretary) — and the six-AU-presidents meeting in which Mays + Clement + Manley + Brawley argued against direct action, while Harry V. Richardson + Frank Cunningham broke ranks to support the students — is canonical first-person testimony on the strategic split among the canonical Atlanta Black-college presidents. Clement's strategic counter-move was to commission the manifesto + raise $12,000 for full-page newspaper publication, hoping that the students would get caught up in "the philosophers of the movement as opposed to activists" — a Whisper-corrected interpretation by King himself.
- **"An Appeal for Human Rights" canonical first-person**: King's first-person account of commissioning Roslyn Pope to write the manifesto with Julian Bond + Charles Black + Marist Dillard — and Pope writing it singlehandedly the night before the deadline at Howard Zinn's house using Zinn's typewriter — is canonical first-person testimony on the under-documented authorship of one of the most-reprinted Movement manifestos.
- **May 17, 1960 march canonical first-person**: King's account of Dr. Mays asking him not to march to the GA Capitol on Brown decision anniversary because Chief Jenkins warned the state troopers were there with billy clubs — and King's quoting back to Mays his own 1960 speech "never sacrifice a principle for peace" — and Mays following him back to confirm to the students himself that he supported the march if they were committed — is canonical first-person testimony on a key intergenerational Movement moment.
- **Atlanta Inquirer founding canonical first-person**: King's account of M. Kasuth Hill walking into his Yates and Milton office in June 1960 with a copy of the *Atlanta Daily World* and proposing they start a rival Black newspaper — and the *Atlanta Inquirer* launching July 4, 1960 with Julian Bond + Charles Black + John Gibson + James Gibson on staff — is canonical first-person testimony on how the Movement organizational infrastructure included independent Black-press creation as a strategic asset.
- **Acid attack canonical first-person**: King's first-person account of being doused with acid (likely sulfuric acid or carbolic acid) by a 57-year-old white assailant outside Manns Brothers grocery in West End Atlanta during the 1960 boycott — and the pool hall across the street emptying of Black men who came to defend him — is canonical first-person testimony on the rare canonical Atlanta near-violent confrontation between Black armed defenders and white attackers.
- **Spelman "if not now, when, if not us, who" canonical first-person**: King's account of debating Roy Wilkins at Spelman April 1960 — and using the canonical Hillel "if not now, when, if not us, who" formulation that turned the Spelman women's support of SNCC — is canonical first-person testimony on the rhetorical mechanics of the 1960 Atlanta organizing.
