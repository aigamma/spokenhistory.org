### 87. Matthew J. Perry Jr.

**Source**: `transcripts/raw/Matthew J. Perry_interview_20250704_235623/`

**Cameraman**: John Bishop
**Subject**: Judge Matthew J. Perry Jr. (b. 1921 Columbia SC; d. 2011, two months after this interview) — canonical SC civil rights attorney + first Black federal judge in South Carolina (US District Court for SC, 1979-2011; the Columbia federal courthouse is now named for him). WWII Army veteran (Camp Van Dorn MS basic training, served in England/France/Belgium/Germany). South Carolina State College law school graduating class 1951 (the second class — diploma privilege was abolished his year, so he was the first SC State law grad to take the SC bar). Practiced in Spartanburg SC 1951-61, then returned to Columbia. Cooperating attorney for NAACP-LDF + NAACP General Counsel. Co-counsel on *Briggs v. Elliott* (1951 SC component of *Brown v. Board* 1954) + *Fleming v. South Carolina Electric and Gas* (1954-56 SC bus desegregation, predating *Browder v. Gayle* Montgomery). Appointed by Jimmy Carter to SC federal bench 1979.

#### Notes for Pass 2

- ***Elmore v. Rice* + *Wrighten v. SC* spring 1947 canonical first-person witness**: Perry's first-person eyewitness account of attending the back-to-back federal court trials of *Elmore v. Rice* (1947 SC white-primary case) and *Wrighten v. South Carolina* (1947 USC law school admission case) — both Thurgood Marshall + Robert Carter + Harold Boulware-litigated — and his decision to pursue law as a career after watching the trials is canonical first-person testimony on the foundational pre-*Brown* SC litigation that produced the SC State law school (which Perry then attended).
- ***Briggs v. Elliott* June 1951 trial canonical first-person witness**: Perry's account of "worming through the crowd" to witness the canonical *Briggs v. Elliott* trial in Charleston SC June 1951, days after his own law school graduation and bar exam — and observing the trial that became one of the 5 consolidated cases in *Brown v. Board of Education* (1954) — is canonical first-person testimony from a contemporary Black law-graduate-witness.
- **Birmingham AL railway sandwich-window 1943 canonical first-person**: Perry's first-person account of being a US Army soldier in uniform forced to order through a kitchen sandwich window at a Birmingham AL train terminal, while Italian POWs were served at the restaurant tables and flirted with by white waitresses — is canonical first-person testimony on a recurring WWII Black soldier humiliation narrative that has been documented across many Black veterans' oral histories but Perry's is unusually vivid.
- **Spartanburg SC 1951 municipal court canonical first-person**: Perry's account of his first 3 cases as a 1951 Spartanburg attorney — where the municipal judge handed his clients maximum sentences ($100 + 30 days) for routine traffic offenses while letting white-attorney-represented clients off with $10 fines — and his innovation of drafting a pre-emptive notice of appeal that he served on the judge during the trial itself — is canonical first-person testimony on the survival mechanics of early-1950s Black attorneys in deep-South municipal courts.
- ***Fleming v. SCE&G* canonical first-person**: Perry's first-person account of co-counseling (with Lincoln Jenkins) Sarah Mae Fleming's June 22, 1954 Columbia SC bus desegregation case after Phil Wittenberg was driven off by a KKK cross-burning on his Sumter County lawn — is canonical first-person testimony on the under-recognized SC bus case that predated Rosa Parks Dec 1, 1955 by 17 months and Browder v. Gayle 1956. Perry's note: "Ms. Rosa Parks famous. But Sarah May Fleming Brown's case was the first one on the record."
- **Moot court for SCOTUS argument canonical first-person**: Perry's account of the Howard University Law School moot court session held before each *Brown v. Board* SCOTUS argument — where Black law professors role-played the sitting Justices — is canonical first-person testimony on the under-documented preparation infrastructure for the canonical SCOTUS civil rights argument tradition.
- **Perry's interview was June 7, 2011 — Perry died two months later (Aug 6, 2011)**. This transcript is a canonical late-life Perry interview, capturing his life-narrative shortly before his death.
