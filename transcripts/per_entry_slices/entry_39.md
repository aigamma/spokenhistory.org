### 39. Ernest Adolphus Finney Jr.

**Source**: `transcripts/raw/Ernest Adolphus Finney_interview_20250704_202033/`
**Interviewer / Date**: Joe Mosnier (Southern Oral History Program at UNC Chapel Hill); videographer John Bishop — 8 June 2011, at Justice Finney's home in Columbia, South Carolina
**Subject**: Justice Ernest Adolphus Finney Jr. (1931–2017) — first African-American Chief Justice of the South Carolina Supreme Court (1994–2000); raised in Smithfield, Virginia and Washington, DC, with multiple moves following his father's teaching positions (the father was Dean of Claflin College, Orangeburg SC); Claflin College undergraduate, South Carolina State University Law School JD 1954 (entered just months before *Brown v. Board*); high school teacher in Conway SC 1954–60; full-time civil rights attorney in Sumter SC from 1960; defended the Friendship Nine (the 1961 Rock Hill Friendship College students who pioneered the "jail no bail" tactic); SC State House of Representatives 1972–76; SC Supreme Court Associate Justice 1985–94; SC Chief Justice 1994–2000; author of the *Abbeville County v. State* decision (the foundational SC school-equity ruling).
**Cross-references**: Finney↔Matthew J. Perry Jr. (the foundational SC civil rights attorney whom Finney came up alongside; later the first Black SC US District Court judge); Finney↔I. DeQuincey Newman (the SC NAACP field secretary referenced throughout); Finney↔Friendship Nine and James T. McCain (the CORE-organized "jail no bail" precedent of February 1961); Finney↔Orangeburg Massacre (his 1968 Civil Rights Commission resignation in protest of the federal non-response to the SC Highway Patrol killing of 3 SC State students).

Pair with any Friendship Nine member transcript.

- **SC Bar 1955 "waiter at the head table" story canonical**: Finney's first SC Bar Association annual meeting (1955) was attended as a waiter at the Ocean Forest Hotel because Black members were not invited to social events despite being members of the bar; he later attended as Chief Justice at the head table. This narrative arc — first as waiter, last as Chief — is canonical for the corpus.
