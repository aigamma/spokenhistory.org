### 71. Julius W. Becton Jr.

**Source**: `transcripts/raw/Julius W. Becton_interview_20250704_223950/`
**Interviewer / Date**: David Cline (Whisper-rendered "David Klein" — recurring) — December 15, 2015
**Subject**: Lt. Gen. Julius Wesley Becton Jr. (b. 1926 Bryn Mawr, PA) — born in the basement of a department store on the Main Line where his father was the janitor; graduated Lower Merion HS in Ardmore, PA (1944), top of class. Joined the Army Air Corps reserves senior year of high school after a recruiter (Tuskegee Airmen recruiter) came to the football team. Failed the astigmatism eye test at Keesler Field, Biloxi, MS in 1944; transferred to McDill Field (aviation engineering); promoted to first sergeant's clerk; selected for OCS at Fort Benning January–August 1945. Combat tour: 93rd Infantry Division on Morotai (Indonesia, Pacific) August 1945–January 1946 with the 31st "Dixie" Division (which flew the Confederate flag) alongside; Korean War 1950–51 with 9th Infantry Regiment, 2nd Infantry Division, breaking out of the Pusan Perimeter at Pohang; took Hill 201 (Korea, Aug 1950, Silver Star); two Purple Hearts; was lead platoon when the Army's first integration order took effect via Col. C.C. Sloan's "put them where they're needed" practice at the 9th Infantry in Korea (a *de facto* integration that preceded Truman's E.O. 9981 enforcement). Later degree from Muhlenberg College, Prairie View A&M (where he was their "5000th graduate" 1960 by convenient timing), Command and General Staff College (Fort Leavenworth), assignments at Fort Bliss, Fort Bragg, Fort Lewis. Rose to Lt. General; later was president of Prairie View A&M.

#### Notes for Pass 2

- ***De facto* integration of 9th Infantry Regiment 1950 canonical first-person**: Becton's first-person account of Col. C.C. Sloan's August 1950 instruction "put them where they're needed" — meaning Sloan ignored racial categories in replacement assignments to the 9th Infantry, two years after E.O. 9981 — is canonical first-person testimony on how the Army's actual integration happened on the ground in Korea before any formal mandate from Washington forced compliance. The first integrated soldier Becton received was a Mexican-American from Texas.
- **Pre-civil-rights-era racial-integration Aberdeen 1948 canonical first-person**: Becton's first-person account of his post commander at Aberdeen Proving Ground reading E.O. 9981 to assembled officers and immediately declaring "as long as I'm here, there'd be no change" — preserving the dual Officer's Club / NCO Club / swimming pool segregation — is canonical first-person testimony on the gap between executive order and enforcement.
- **Van Horn Texas 1948 canonical first-person**: Becton's account of being chased out of a Van Horn TX drugstore by an armed sheriff while in uniform, followed the next morning by his soldiers saving two white women in a car accident — and the sheriff's resulting respect — is canonical first-person testimony on the *visible competence as Southern integration tool* pattern that ran parallel to but separate from civil rights activism.
- **Tuskegee Institute origin canonical first-person**: Becton's reference back to Carlisle Barracks 1925 study (deeming Black soldiers unfit for technical/leadership roles) — and his own 1944 OCS experience under that lingering institutional prejudice — is canonical first-person testimony on the long arc from the 1925 pseudoscience to the post-1965 black-general-officer pipeline.
- **Drill-team integration 1950 canonical first-person**: Becton's account of being told as a Lt. with the 3rd Bn 9th Infantry drill team at Fort Lewis 1950 that white soldiers couldn't join "because they'll break up the parents in the color" — is canonical first-person testimony on the absurdity of separate-but-equal in performance units.
- **First-person Tuskegee Airman test failure**: Becton's account of the new vision-testing technology at Keesler 1944 that defeated his memorized-chart eye-test trick is canonical first-person testimony on how the Tuskegee Airmen pipeline's "wash out" rate was partly produced by technological screening that disproportionately caught Black recruits.
