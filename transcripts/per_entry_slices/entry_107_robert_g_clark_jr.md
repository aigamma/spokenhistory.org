**Interviewer / Date**: Dr. John Dittmer (canonical MS-Movement historian; *Local People*) — Thursday March 14, 2013, Pickens MS (the Clark family home, where speaker was born ~1929 and has lived ever since)
**Cameraman**: John Bishop
**Subject**: Robert G. Clark Jr. (b. ~1929 Pickens MS, Holmes County), canonical first Black to be elected to the Mississippi House of Representatives in the 20th century (elected 1967, sat in 1968 after a federal-court fight over JP Love's contestation; sole Black member of the 122-member MS House for the first 8 years; ultimately served until 2003). Family background: descendant of slaves on the same Pickens MS Holmes County plantation his great-grandfather had been a slave on — and which the family bought from the former slave master after Emancipation, and still owns today. As an 11-year-old in 1940 he was jailed overnight in Lexington MS with his father (on the trumped-up murder charges that ultimately resulted in his father's acquittal on grounds of self-defense, defended by a Lexington white attorney). Education through the segregated 2-teacher 8-grade Sandy Flat school (3 miles walked rain-sleet-or-snow) → Mt. Olive Vocational High School (where his uncle was principal) → Holmes County Training School (now Williams-Sullivan School, Durant MS — Clark walked from Pickens to catch the 2 AM train to Durant every Monday morning) → Jackson College for Negro Teachers (Lynch Street, Jackson — now Jackson State University; Clark arrived with less than $5 from his aunts after walking + bussing from Pickens, was almost sent home by President Jacob L. Reddix but offered a custodial-and-women's-dormitory-garbage-removal work-aid arrangement; became the first track scholarship recipient at Jackson State 1949+) → Michigan State University master's degree (after being warned away from the dean of students who told him a Black student could not pursue both a teaching degree and law). Taught at Humphreys County Training School in Louise MS for 7 years (where the canonical 1956-57 Belzoni-Meal-barber-shop death of his fellow voter-registration activist Rubin/Rubin Lee occurred while Clark was in the next chair) — fired in 1958 after telling the local superintendent that "the white parents need to explain to their children that it is fine" for Black and white children to attend school together. Fired again in 1966 from Holmes County after agitating for an Adult Education program; the Holmes County Board redrew the Superintendent of Education position from elected to appointed via local-and-private legislative act specifically to block Clark's announced 1967 candidacy. Clark ran instead for the state legislative seat, won as an Independent (Marian Wright Edelman was his lead lawyer in defending JP Love's contestation); served continuously until 2003 — last 11 years (1992-2003) as Speaker Pro Tempore. Canonical 1982 Mississippi Education Reform Act (compulsory school attendance + early childhood / kindergarten education) chief House sponsor under Governor William Winter + Speaker Buddie Newman.
