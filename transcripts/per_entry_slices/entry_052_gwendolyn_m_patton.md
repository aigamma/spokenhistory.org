**Subject**: Dr. Gwendolyn M. Patton — Montgomery AL-born; raised by her grandmother ("Mommy") on her mother's side and her Patton paternal grandparents; teenager during the 1955-56 Montgomery bus boycott; Detroit Inkster high-school years (briefly); Tuskegee Institute 1961-67 (interrupted by tuberculosis hospitalization at Benson Sanatorium Lafayette AL January 1963 to March 1964 — canonical 14-month-segregated-facility first-person); foundational woman leader of TIAL (Tuskegee Institute Advancement League) and Tuskegee student body president 1965-66 then vice-president 1966-67; foundational woman organizer of the Selma-to-Montgomery March's "march that wouldn't turn around" Tuskegee contingent March 1965 (1,500 Tuskegee students plus the cafeteria-workers-organized food); Sharp (Southern Human Relations Project) organizer who underwrote the canonical March 31 – April 2, 1967 Tuskegee Black Power Conference; mysterious near-fatal February 10, 1967 single-car accident on Shorter AL highway in which her axle inexplicably broke — leading to 6+ months at John Andrew Memorial Hospital on the Tuskegee campus where she resisted Dr. Hume's amputation recommendation; subsequent NYC Medical Committee for Human Rights surgery 1967-68; Mary Kochiyama / Julia Wright / Bettina Aptheker / Communist Party / Social Workers Party / Progressive Labor Party NYC milieu 1967-68; longtime SNCC organizer in NYC and Atlanta.

#### Notes for Pass 2

- **Full read**: This transcript was fully read end-to-end. The transcript is very long (~190 KB) and dense.
- **TIAL canonical first-person**: Patton is the foundational woman leader of TIAL (Tuskegee Institute Advancement League), one of the few SNCC-affiliated organizations that maintained a distinct local identity (a principle Patton inherited from Montgomery's MIA refusing to be subsumed by SCLC in 1957). Pair with Sammy Younge Jr. (the murdered TIAL co-organizer, January 3, 1966) and Kathleen Neal Cleaver references.
- **"The march that wouldn't turn around" canonical first-person**: Patton's account of organizing 1,500 Tuskegee students plus cafeteria-workers-organized box-lunches into the March 9-10, 1965 caravan to Montgomery during the Selma-to-Montgomery campaign — and George Ware's reading of the Judge Frank Johnson injunction language to find that it didn't enjoin a Tuskegee-to-Montgomery march, only Selma-to-Montgomery — is canonical first-person material on this often-overlooked second-tier Selma-March event.
- **Benson Sanatorium 14-month segregation canonical first-person**: Patton's account of being hospitalized for tuberculosis at the segregated Benson Sanatorium in Lafayette AL January 1963 – March 1964 — including her individual movements (library access, kitchen menu posting, classical piano in the white sitting room) that gradually broke down the segregation regime — is one of the most-detailed first-person sources in the corpus on the texture of medical-facility Jim Crow.
- **Tuskegee Black Power Conference 1967 canonical first-person**: Patton organized and underwrote the first contemporary Black Power Conference at Tuskegee in April 1967, predating the better-known Newark NJ Black Power Conference of July 1967. Pass 2 should treat this as canonical and add to the ground-truth corpus.
- **Lowndes County / John Hulett canonical first-person**: Patton's framing of the LCFO's deliberate choice to focus on sheriff / Board of Education / tax assessor / coroner rather than state representatives or DNC delegates — because "those were the positions closest to the people" — is canonical first-person material on the LCFO's anti-DNC-vote-vs-local-power strategy. Pair with Stokely Carmichael / John Hulett references.
- **The MIA-refused-to-become-an-SCLC-chapter canonical thesis**: Patton's framing that the MIA refused to be subsumed into the SCLC at its 1957 founding (at her family's church) is canonical first-person material on the foundational SNCC/SCLC organizational-autonomy tension that would shape decades of movement strategy debates.
- **The "you should not submerge" organizing principle canonical first-person**: Patton's articulation of the principle that movement organizers should not absorb local-community-organization identities into their own organization — which she traces back to MIA's refusal to become SCLC — is canonical first-person material on a foundational SNCC organizing axiom.
- **Aunt Chick "no whites in my house" 1961 canonical first-person**: Patton's account of her Aunt Chick's "I don't want any white people in my house" reaction to the Mother's-Day-Massacre Freedom Riders sitting in her living room — and Aunt Chick's reasoning ("As long as I have to go through the back door of white folks' houses, they're not to enter the front door of my home") — is canonical first-person material on the Black-Southern-middle-class racial-pride logic.
- **The 1967 mysterious axle-broke car accident canonical first-person**: Patton's February 10, 1967 single-car accident in Shorter AL — in which her axle inexplicably broke without an impact — and the subsequent 6+ month hospitalization at John Andrew Memorial Hospital where she refused Dr. Hume's amputation recommendation — is canonical first-person material on the suspicious incident that interrupted her TIAL leadership.
- **NYC Movement-Left milieu 1967-68 canonical first-person**: Patton's NYC sojourn during her medical recovery (Mary Kochiyama, Julia Wright, Bettina Aptheker, the Communist Party, Social Workers Party, Progressive Labor Party, the $1199 union organizing) is canonical first-person material on the BPP-era multi-racial New Left coalition work.
