### 78. Linda Fuller Degelmann

**Source**: `transcripts/raw/Linda Fuller Degelmann_interview_20250704_231240/`
**Interviewer / Date**: Emilye Crosby — May 28, 2013, Americus, Georgia
**Cameraman**: John Bishop
**Subject**: Linda Fuller Degelmann (b. 1941 Birmingham, AL; raised Tuscaloosa, AL) — wife of Millard Fuller (b. 1935 — d. 2009, Habitat for Humanity co-founder). Daughter of Caldwell Electric Company owners. Married Millard 1959; lived Montgomery 1959-65 while Millard + Morris Dees built Fuller & Dees Marketing. The couple housed Selma-to-Montgomery marchers in their Montgomery home March 1965 (which drew neighbor harassment + KKK phone calls). After a 1965 marriage crisis triggered by Millard + Dees's "millionaires by 30" obsession, they sold all wealth, joined Koinonia Farm (Sumter County, GA) in 1968 under Clarence Jordan's leadership, lived intentional Christian community with partnership housing / partnership farming / partnership industries. Founded Habitat for Humanity 1976 in Americus after returning from 3 years building 120 houses in Mbandaka, Zaire (now DRC); built Habitat to ~100 countries + ~1M houses by 2005. Forced out of Habitat 2005 in board dispute; founded the Fuller Center for Housing 2005.

#### Notes for Pass 2

- **Koinonia Farm 1968-73 canonical first-person**: Fuller's first-person account of arriving at Koinonia in Aug 1968 when only the Jordans + one other family remained — and building up "partnership housing / partnership industries / partnership farming" with Clarence Jordan that became the proof-of-concept for Habitat for Humanity — is canonical first-person testimony on the under-documented 1968-69 Koinonia revival that bridged the 1950s armed-attack-on-Koinonia era with the 1976 Habitat-for-Humanity founding.
- **Freedom Rides 1961 Montgomery bus station canonical first-person (second-hand)**: Fuller's account of Millard + Morris Dees going to the Montgomery bus station in May 1961 (out of curiosity) and being shocked at the Freedom Rider beatings — which she identifies as the "180-degree conversion moment" for both men away from KKK political affiliation — is canonical first-person testimony on a now-canonical moral-conversion narrative.
- **First Baptist Church Americus Christmas 1965 canonical first-person**: Fuller's first-person account of the integrated Koinonia group attempting to attend a Christmas service at First Baptist Church Americus where the Georgia Baptist Convention head was preaching — and being ejected on the basis that "the federal government doesn't own this church" — including Clarence Jordan's "look at all that light on the outside and it's dark on the inside" remark — is canonical first-person testimony.
- **Unadilla, GA jailing canonical first-person**: Fuller's first-person account of being arrested with Millard for visiting a Black family in Unadilla GA (one of the kids was challenging school segregation) — and the police chief releasing them but warning "we're not responsible for you when you leave town" — followed by the FBI involvement after they hid in a motel — is canonical first-person testimony on the post-1964-Civil-Rights-Act ongoing harassment pattern.
- **Maranatha Baptist Plains canonical first-person**: Fuller mentions that Jimmy Carter "twisted her arm" to join Maranatha Baptist Church Plains after Carter joined Habitat — is canonical first-person testimony on Carter's recruitment style.
- **Bob Zellner Huntingdon College sociology class cross-corpus**: Speaker references Bob Zellner's canonical Huntingdon sociology class that took the students to meet Dr. King — which is recurring across the Movement memoir corpus.
