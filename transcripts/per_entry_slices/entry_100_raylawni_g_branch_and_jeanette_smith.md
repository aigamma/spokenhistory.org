### 100. Raylawni G. Branch and Jeanette Smith

**Source**: `transcripts/raw/Raylawni G. Branch and Jeanette Smith_interview_20250705_004222/`
**Interviewer / Date**: Emilye Crosby — December 1, 2015, University of Southern Mississippi Center for Oral History and Cultural Heritage, Hattiesburg MS
**Cameraman**: not specified in opening narration
**Subject**: Two-speaker joint interview with Mrs. Jeanette Smith (b. January 1, 1940, Jones County / Soso MS — direct descendant of Captain Newton Knight via Newton + Rachel of the canonical Free State of Jones) and Mrs. Raylawni G. Branch (b. September 1, 1941, Hattiesburg MS — mixed Cajun / Black / Native American ancestry). Both were the first two Black women to integrate the University of Southern Mississippi in September 1965 (then "Mississippi Southern College"), arriving after the Ole Miss / James Meredith breakthrough had absorbed the worst of the legal-political shockwave. The interview is conducted in the McCain Library on the USM campus — named for the segregationist USM president William D. McCain who delayed admitting Black students through the early 1960s, and who refused Clyde Kennard's three application attempts (1959-1961, with Kennard ultimately framed for chicken-feed theft via false-witness testimony from Johnny Lee Roberts, sentenced to 7 years at Parchman, developed cancer there, died January 1966). Pre-integration: Branch was an active member of the Hattiesburg NAACP youth chapter (founded 1946, oldest in MS); registered to vote with Forrest County circuit clerk Theron Lynd (one of the canonical Mississippi voter-registration obstruction figures) accompanied by Vernon Dahmer, JC Fairley, and James Bourne; was the first Black woman hired by the Hattiesburg telephone company, Sears, and Big Yank clothing manufacturer (the latter through a "permanent press" / piece-work / union-contract gambit that organized the night shift). Branch also helped found the canonical 1968 freedom-school / Head Start substrate by literally putting children on a Greyhound bus to Washington DC and Lady Bird Johnson's care to force the establishment of the Hattiesburg Head Start program. The interview is also a canonical first-person source on the Clyde Kennard frame-up — Branch and JC Fairley flew with Dick Gregory + Charles Evers (Medgar Evers' brother) to retrieve the false-witness Johnny Lee Roberts in Chicago to attempt to get him to recant; the retrieval failed but Jerry Mitchell of the Clarion-Ledger later got Roberts to tell the true story decades later. Branch went on to St. John's Episcopal School of Nursing in NYC; her husband Mr. Branch was a Meharry-trained Black physician (originally from Memphis) who built his own clinic in Hattiesburg, integrated Forrest General Hospital via federal Hill-Burton Act complaint, and delivered babies in Hattiesburg through the Civil-Rights / Medicare-era integration of medical care.

**Cross-references**: Captain Newton Knight (Free State of Jones); Rachel (Knight's enslaved-then-spouse); Victoria E. Bynum (author of *The Free State of Jones*, referenced as "Dr. Biden"); Vernon Dahmer (recurring across 10+ corpus transcripts; killed Jan 10, 1966); Ellie Dahmer (#36 in this corpus, Vernon's widow); Clyde Kennard (recurring across multiple corpus transcripts as canonical Mississippi-Higher-Ed-integration martyr); Theron Lynd (Forrest County circuit clerk; canonical voter-registration obstruction figure, cross-corpus with Guyot #76 and Connor #96); JC Fairley (Hattiesburg NAACP president); James Bourne; Medgar Evers (canonical, killed Jun 12, 1963); Charles Evers (Medgar's brother); Dick Gregory (canonical activist-comedian); William D.
