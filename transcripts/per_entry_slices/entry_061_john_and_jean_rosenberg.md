**Source**: `transcripts/raw/John and Jean Rosenberg_interview_20250704_214914/`
**Interviewer / Date**: David Klein (Virginia Tech History Department / SOHP); videographer John Bishop — 15 August 2013 (joint interview with John on Channel 2, Jean on Channel 1)
**Subject**: John Rosenberg — German-Jewish refugee (1931 Magdeburg, Germany; family arrived in NYC February 22, 1940 on the last ship out before WWII); father a teacher in the Jewish school system; Spartanburg SC and Gastonia NC childhood; Duke University 1949-53 (during the Jewish-quota era; lost his tuition scholarship after his sponsor C. Park died); US Air Force navigator 1953-56 in England (the canonical "Abe Jenkins train-segregation moment" in Washington DC return-from-England 1955 — Jenkins had to walk to the back of the train, and Rosenberg thought "this is wrong; maybe somewhere along the way I'll have a chance to do something about that"); UNC Chapel Hill Law School 1959-62 (where he became friends with Julius Chambers); foundational Civil Rights Division Department of Justice attorney 1962-70 (Mississippi section under Bob Owen — Mississippi voting-rights, public-accommodations, and police-brutality cases; canonical first-person on the Mississippi voting-records-microfilm strategy under John Doar's evidentiary-pattern-analysis leadership). // Jean Rosenberg (née Shelton) — Philadelphia PA-born (1943); Quaker Pennsylvania background (Abington Friends Meeting); refused ARA drills in her Abington High School (labeled "communist dupe" alongside Mulholland #60.); Wilmington College OH 1961-62 → transferred to Earlham College IN 1962-65 (where her dorm pre-assigned her a Black roommate from the Bronx as test-case to see how her hallmates would react); Civil Rights Division Research Analyst June 1965-1970 (under John Doar; recruited by Doar after his Earlham convocation visit; the canonical 1965-onward Cointelpro-era research-analyst era).

- **Partial read**: ~58% of the 123 KB transcript was read (cap-truncated). Pass 2 must read the remaining 42% covering the Rosenbergs' post-1970 Civil Rights Division work, their move to Prestonsburg KY to found Appalachian Research and Defense Fund (Appalred) — the foundational Eastern Kentucky legal-aid organization — and their lifelong Appalachian-Kentucky work.
- **Civil Rights Division Mississippi section canonical first-person**: John Rosenberg's account of the 1962-70 Mississippi section work — including the foundational John Doar evidentiary-pattern-analysis voting-records strategy, the Hartman Turnbow self-defense / KKK-bombing case, the Fannie Lou Hamer Winona MS jail-beating prosecution (acquittal December 1963 — the day after Oswald was shot by Ruby), the Theron Lynd contempt-of-court proceedings in Hattiesburg, the Charles Atlas Louisiana cotton-ginning conspiracy case, and the foundational Mississippi voting-records-microfilm strategy — is canonical first-person material on the foundational Civil Rights Division operation. Pair with Marian Wright Edelman / Greenberg #56 transcripts.
- **John Doar / Bobby Kennedy "we need four more lawyers" canonical first-person**: Rosenberg's account of how the Eisenhower-to-Kennedy transition resulted in John Doar staying on as Civil Rights Division chief under Bobby Kennedy — and Bobby Kennedy asking "how many do you need?" / Burke Marshall's "four" reply (since the judiciary-committee Senator Eastland-blockade meant they couldn't get 30 lawyers anyway) — is canonical first-person material on the foundational 1961-65 Civil Rights Division institutional scale.
- **Foot Campbell Yazoo County 38-pistol canonical first-person**: Rosenberg's account of being summoned into Yazoo County MS Circuit Clerk "Foot" Campbell's office, listening to Campbell's lynching reminiscences, and then having Campbell pull a 38 pistol out of his drawer and shoot it into the floor in front of Rosenberg's foot (with the FBI in the next room photographing voting records) — "the only time anything like that ever happened to me" — is canonical first-person material on the foundational pre-Voting-Rights-Act registrar-intimidation tactic.
- **Dallas County 1966 recount canonical first-person**: Rosenberg's account of the foundational 1966 Selma Sheriff Jim Clark recount case — including Jean and Dorothy Shelton Landsberg in the Sheriff's office watching ballot boxes for a week alongside Jim Clark's deputies who tried to romance them and gave them "never" buttons — is canonical first-person material on the foundational first-post-Voting-Rights-Act Black-voter-registration election outcome in the Deep South.
- **Holocaust-refugee-as-civil-rights-attorney canonical first-person**: John Rosenberg's framing that his commitment to civil rights came from "having been born in Germany and seeing and thinking about the Holocaust history of ours and the terrible genocide... and seeing really in having been in the service with people who were Black and thinking that this caste system is wrong in this country, being very proud of this country" — is canonical first-person material on the foundational Jewish-refugee-to-civil-rights-attorney trajectory.
