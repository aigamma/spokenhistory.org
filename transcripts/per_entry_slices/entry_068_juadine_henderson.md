### 68. Juadine Henderson

**Source**: `transcripts/raw/Juadine Henderson_interview_20250704_222215/`  
*Layer 5 removed 7 low-impact phantom rendering rows (whisper renderings not present in raw).*
**Interviewer / Date**: Emilye Crosby (Whisper-rendered "Heavy Crossy" / "Emily Crosby") — December 10, 2015, Library of Congress, Washington DC
**Cameraman**: John Bishop (Whisper-rendered "John Fisher" — canonical recurring Whisper-error for "Bishop")
**Subject**: Juadine Henderson — born Batesville, Mississippi, Panola County; daughter of Lyora Morris and Hugo Henderson. Family was independent landowning Black farmers in Section 7 (Olive Ray community) in northern Panola County (which has a unique Black-land-ownership pattern — half Panola is in the Delta, half is Hill Country, and Black families "all over the place" owned their own land, breaking the sharecropper-dominated Delta norm). Activated by SNCC's Frank Smith arrival Thanksgiving 1962; attended June 1963 Greenwood voter-registration workshop while Medgar Evers was killed; canvassed Panola County 1963–64. Was 18 when she joined SNCC's Mississippi Freedom Labor Union / Delta Ministry Freedom Corps and worked Bolivar County (Rosedale, Cleveland MS, Shaw); also did Northern fundraising trips for the Delta Ministry. Later attended Bishop College in Texas. Wore a natural / afro hair from 1965, predating *Ebony's* September 1966 "All Natural" cover.

#### Notes for Pass 2

- **Section 7 / Panola County Black land-ownership canonical first-person**: Henderson's account of her family farming their own 80-acre Farm Security Administration government-purchased plot in Section 7 (Olive Ray community) — and her observation that South Panola County had widespread Black land-ownership unlike the Delta plantation-sharecropping norm — is canonical first-person testimony on an under-documented pattern of Black landowning that gave Panola County its unique Civil Rights Movement infrastructure base. Pair with Burns #70 same iteration (Mileston, Holmes County FSA land ownership).
- **"Christmas Attucks" → Crispus Attucks Whisper-error**: One of the most surreal Whisper errors observed in the corpus, alongside Lowery's "Star Spank Obama" → "Star-Spangled Banner" (transcript #66).
- **Greenwood June 1963 voter-registration workshop canonical first-person**: Henderson's first-person account of being at the Greenwood SNCC office on June 12, 1963 when news arrived that Medgar Evers had been killed — and the energy-shift "the next morning we went in to the SNCC office. And it was a very different energy" — is canonical first-person bridge testimony between the Evers assassination and the Mississippi Freedom Summer organizing that followed.
- **Mississippi Freedom Labor Union canonical first-person**: Henderson's account of Bolivar County / Rosedale MS labor organizing summer 1965–66 — including the "$3/day, 30 cents/hour, 10 hour days" plantation wage structure that the Mississippi Freedom Labor Union challenged — is canonical first-person testimony on the under-documented MFLU efforts.
- **Strike City canonical first-person**: Henderson worked with Isaac Foster, one of the Strike City founders, and her account of the post-Strike-City Greenville Air Force Base tent-encampment context is canonical.
- **Women-armed-at-mass-meetings canonical first-person**: Henderson's account of West Camp Missionary Baptist Church women all "petting their purses" (which contained pistols) in unison when whites approached the church for a voter registration meeting — and her later observation that "the women walked with pistols in their purses, even to church" — is canonical first-person testimony on women's armed self-defense in the Mississippi Movement, supplementing the better-known Robert F. Williams / Deacons for Defense narrative.
- **Sammy Davis Jr.-not-mentioned**: Unlike Lowery #66 same iteration, no Sammy Davis Jr. reference in this transcript.
