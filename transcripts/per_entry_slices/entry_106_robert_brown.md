**Interviewer / Date**: David Cline (Virginia Tech / SOHP UNC Chapel Hill) — October 1, 2013, High Point NC
**Cameraman**: John Bishop (Media Generation / UCLA); with Elaine Nichols (Smithsonian curator) also present
**Subject**: Robert J. Brown (b. February 1935, High Point NC), founder of B&C Associates (1960) — one of the first Black-owned public-relations consulting firms in the United States, with F.W. Woolworth as its first major client. Family background: raised by maternal grandmother Nelly Marshall Brown (née Kendall, born Anson County NC), whose father Joseph Kendall Sr. was a slave used to build the NC railroads — a fact Brown only fully grasped when his grandmother brought him and his brother by bus through Fayetteville NC c. 1943 specifically to see the old Fayetteville slave market where Joseph Kendall had been last sold. Educated through Lincoln Street School → William Penn High School (Quaker-founded High Point Black high school, where Mary McLeod Bethune spent an afternoon counseling Brown as her student escort, and where Langston Hughes + Morehouse Dean visited as alumni speakers). Took the High Point police examination in his sophomore year at North Carolina A&T → joined HPPD as first integrating-arrests Black officer (arrested whites despite the unwritten "Black officers arrest Black suspects" rule) → recruited into federal civil-rights / mafia-investigation work in NYC (testified in the Vito Genovese case + other major mafia cases through ~1958-60). Quit December 1960 and founded B&C Associates in High Point NC; first major client was F.W. Woolworth (which approached him in early 1961 after the Greensboro sit-ins to advise on integrating their stores and corporate structure). Through F.W. Woolworth's board (John J. McCloy + Fred Kirby of Allegheny Corp), Brown became the lead PR consultant on race-relations crisis management for major American corporations through the 1960s — addressing executive teams + boards on how to navigate the unprecedented post-Civil-Rights-Act business landscape. Simultaneously, Brown funded and brokered SCLC operations from behind the scenes — paying for hotel meeting bills + bail money + travel + logistics for Dr. King, Rev. Wyatt Tee Walker, and SCLC operations across the South. Personally drove around the South testing integration of major hotels (canonical Memphis Peabody Hotel suite-rental test) on behalf of SCLC. After Dr. King's April 4, 1968 assassination, Brown picked up the body in Memphis with Mrs. King; hosted Robert Kennedy + Ethel + Sidney Poitier + others in a Hyatt Regency Atlanta suite the night before King's funeral. Then joined Nixon's 1968 campaign as Bob Finch's personal recruit; rode the canonical Ohio "Bring Us Together" whistle-stop train (the last whistle-stop tour in American presidential history). Nixon called him personally in late November 1968 to make him a White House Special Assistant — the first Black principal advisor with full cabinet-meeting access. Served Jan 1969-Apr 1973; led Nixon-era Black-capitalism initiatives + Black-Republican outreach; survived multiple Cabinet-secretary-and-business-establishment attempts to remove him with direct presidential backing. Post-White-House: Coretta Scott King's 1986 South Africa trip (Brown personally arranged Mrs. King's visas through SA Ambassador Piet Koornhof; personally arranged + funded the Mandela grandchildren's 7-year Boston University education after Winnie Mandela's request; met Nelson Mandela inside Pollsmoor Prison via a special grant from PW Botha — the first non-family meeting with Mandela in 27 years).
