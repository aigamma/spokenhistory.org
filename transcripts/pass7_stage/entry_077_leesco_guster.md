### Proposal 1: *NAACP v. Claiborne Hardware Co.*, 458 U.S. 886 (1982)

- **Name**: NAACP v. Claiborne Hardware Co.
- **Role**: Unanimous Supreme Court decision (8-0; Justice Marshall recused; opinion by Justice Stevens) holding that the Port Gibson, Mississippi NAACP boycott of white merchants (1966-72) was protected political expression under the First Amendment, even though it caused economic harm to the boycotted businesses and included elements of intimidation.
- **Why it belongs**: One of the most significant civil-rights SCOTUS decisions of the 1980s; foundational First Amendment protection for economic boycotts as political action; arose directly from the grassroots Port Gibson movement documented across multiple corpus entries; Guster is a named co-defendant with first-person testimony. Not in any standard civil-rights-leader biographical corpus but is a landmark case that recurs across SW Mississippi entries.
- **Transcript evidence**: Speaker confirms named-defendant status; describes her testimony about Allen Motors in the Claiborne Hardware trial; and recalls the SCOTUS outcome. Corrected transcript: "How did I feel when it went to the Supreme Court in 1982?"

### Proposal 2: Rabbit Foot Minstrels (F. S. Wolcott Original Rabbit Foot Minstrels, Port Gibson, MS, ca. 1914-1959)

- **Name**: F. S. Wolcott Original Rabbit Foot Minstrels (also: Pat Chappelle's Rabbit's Foot Comedy Co., founded ca. 1900)
- **Role**: Canonical Black tent-show vaudeville / minstrel company headquartered in Port Gibson, Mississippi from approximately 1914 to 1959 under impresario Fred Swift Wolcott. Featured Ma Rainey, Bessie Smith, Ida Cox, Big Joe Turner, Rufus Thomas, and Louis Jordan. Foundational institution in Mississippi Delta blues and Black vaudeville tradition; a primary early-career launching pad for major Black artists.
- **Why it belongs**: Port Gibson is the company's historical home base — meaning this entry's subject-community cultural backdrop includes one of the most important Black entertainment institutions in American history. ### Proposal 3: Rudy Shields (~1932-c. 1980)

- **Name**: Rudy Shields
- **Role**: Canonical Southwest Mississippi NAACP/Movement field organizer; came to Port Gibson with Charles Evers 1965-66; coordinated voter-registration canvassing across Claiborne, Yazoo, Natchez, and Hattiesburg areas; later lived in Yazoo City, MS where he died (~late 1970s). Known as "brave" and "not scared of nothing" (Guster); carried both a shotgun and pistol during the Rocky Springs canvassing operations.
