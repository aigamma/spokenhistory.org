**Proposals (0–3 not yet in corpus):**

**Proposal A — Reverend Solomon S. Seay Sr.**

- **Name:** Solomon S. Seay Sr.
- **Role:** Montgomery AME Zion minister (~1880s–1973); pastor of Mount Zion AME Zion Church, Montgomery AL; canonical host of the first Montgomery Bus Boycott mass meeting (December 1955); coiner of the "doodle bird" / "doodlebug" stand-your-ground folk-analogy deployed at the May 1961 First Baptist Church overnight mob-standoff.
- **Why they belong:** Named in at least two corpus entries (this transcript + Patton #52.23 cross-reference); canonical Montgomery Movement religious leadership figure; his "doodle bird" teaching is a canonical first-person idiom documented by Jones as a direct childhood memory. High cross-entry recurrence. Whisper-prone variants: "Reverend Say," "Reverend Sey," "Reverend Saye."
- **Transcript evidence:** "I was just going to say that Reverend Solomon S. Seay Sr said that night to me… And he said, we're going to do like the doodle bird." Also: "I was with Reving Sey, Sey, who was… had the first meeting for the Montgomery Bus Boycott."

**Proposal B — Montgomery Gospel Trio**

- **Name:** Montgomery Gospel Trio
- **Role:** Foundational student-led Montgomery Black-youth gospel-and-freedom-songs group formed at Alabama State Laboratory High School (~1956-57); sang at MIA mass-meeting fundraising teas throughout the boycott era and into the early 1960s; performed at Carnegie Hall (NYC) and contributed to one of the first recorded civil rights song albums; members included Jamila Jones, Gladys Carter, and Hendrix (married McCans); the group is distinguished from the SNCC Freedom Singers (formed 1962) and represents the pre-SNCC youth-song tradition.
- **Why they belong:** First-person primary source for the often-overlooked youth-song component of the Montgomery Movement; Whisper-prone aliases include "Montgomery Trail," "Montgomery Tree," "Montgomery Gospel Tree" — high-damage if left uncataloged in the ground-truth corpus.
- **Transcript evidence:** "We were the Montgomery Gospel Trio. We did not have a name… when we got there, they said, for this album, you got to have a name. And we debated and debate about what could be our name and came up with the Montgomery Gospel Trio."

**Proposal C — Harambee Singers**

- **Name:** Harambee Singers
- **Role:** Atlanta-based Black women's a cappella ensemble co-founded ca. 1968 by Bernice Johnson Reagon, Jamila Jones, and Matti Casey Pierce; performed Pan-African / Black Power-era repertoire ("Hands Off Nkrumah," "We'll Move On Over You"); predecessor ensemble to Sweet Honey in the Rock (founded 1973 by Reagon); distinct from SNCC Freedom Singers; documented first in this transcript as a co-founder's first-person account.
- **Why they belong:** Canonical bridge between 1960s civil-rights-movement song tradition and 1970s Pan-Africanist / Black-feminist music organizing; Whisper-prone aliases are extraordinarily varied: "Harlem Bay Singles," "Parabai singers," "Army singers," "Harry" — four distinct Whisper renderings within a single transcript. High-damage cross-corpus risk if not cataloged.
- **Transcript evidence:** "myself, along with another singer… Matti Casey Pierce, Pierce Now, went to Bernie's house knocked on the door and said, we'd like to just continue to sing… And thus far, we started the Harambee Singers."
