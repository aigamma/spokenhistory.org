### Section 3 — Residual Ground-Truth Corpus Proposals

The following figures are referenced prominently in this transcript and are **not** present in the 140-entry `civil_rights_facts.json` corpus (confirmed by search — no matches for Sterling Brown, Willa B. Player, Sidney Kaplan, Bennett Belles, Hobart Jarrett, Bernard Bell, or Eugene Terry):

**Proposal 1: Dr. Willa B. Player (1909–2003)**

- **Role:** Bennett College president 1955–66; first Black woman to lead a fully-accredited four-year liberal arts college in the US; privately supported the Bennett Belles' participation in the 1960 Greensboro sit-ins; drove personally to the Greensboro jail to vouch for arrested students; survived a cross-burning at her official residence during the 1960 sit-in period.
- **Why she belongs:** Player is one of the most-referenced figures in this transcript and is the canonical HBCU women's-college presidential figure for the 1955–66 sit-in era. Her institutional role (privately-funded college president = independence from state retaliation that public-college presidents faced) is a key historiographical distinction. Terry's account is the most detailed first-person source in the corpus on Player.
- **Transcript evidence:** "Dr. Willa B. Player was told and Dr. Willa B. Player said she couldn't tell us not to her girls... She got in her car and drove down... these are my girls... There had been a crossburn. She did have a crossburned at the college president's house." Multiple extended passages across the second half of the transcript.
- **Confidence:** high — canonical historical figure; biographical facts cross-referenced against Wikipedia and consistent with Pass 4 fact-check.

**Proposal 2: Sterling A. Brown (1901–89)**

- **Role:** Howard University English professor 1929–69; foundational Black-literature scholar (*The Negro in American Fiction*, 1937; *Negro Poetry and Drama*, 1937); Harlem Renaissance / New Negro era foundational figure; the pipeline node who routed Terry, Thelwell, Bernard Bell, and Eugene Terry to Sidney Kaplan at UMass Amherst, thereby generating the founding faculty cohort of the UMass W.E.B. Du Bois Department of Afro-American Studies.
- **Why he belongs:** Brown's graduate-student routing pipeline (Howard → UMass Amherst via Kaplan) is documented in at least two transcripts in this corpus (entry #34 Thelwell, entry #40 Terry) and constitutes a foundational institutional-origin story for Black Studies as a discipline. Brown is not a "Movement" figure in the SNCC/SCLC sense but is a key intellectual infrastructure node whose absence from the corpus creates a gap in the Black Studies provenance chain.
- **Transcript evidence:** "Sterling A. Brown out of Howard University had been sending his graduate students... You really want to study with Sidney Kaplan... He had sent us all there. Sterling had sent us all there. We used to say we were Sterling's old gang and folk there."
- **Confidence:** high — canonical Howard scholar; biographical facts consistent across Passes 1–4 and cross-referenced with Thelwell #34.

**Proposal 3: The Bennett Belles (February 1960 Greensboro sit-ins cohort)**

- **Role:** The group of Bennett College women who planned and conducted the February 1, 1960 Greensboro lunch counter sit-ins in parallel with — and in the parallel-planning thesis, prior to and in coordination with — the more-famous A&T Four. Named members: Esther Terry (née Alexander), Roslyn Smith, Gwendolyn Mackel, Shirley Dismuke, Dolores Finger, Gloria Brown, Linda Brown. Dr. Hobart Jarrett and Dr. Willa B. Player were the faculty/administrative figures who shaped the planning.
- **Why they belong:** The existing corpus entry for "Greensboro sit-ins" (civil_rights_facts.json line 101–104) attributes the sit-ins exclusively to the A&T Four and does not mention the Bennett College women. Terry's transcript is the primary first-person source for the parallel-planning thesis, which is a significant historiographical correction to the spontaneous-A&T narrative. The Smithsonian/LoC publication context makes this omission particularly consequential.
- **Transcript evidence:** "students were planning and talking about a boycott... it had come, that action had been born of a plan, that had been carefully, carefully considered and deliberated beginning here on Bennett's campus with the girls... Gwen Mackel... said I was in Woolworths... because we were there... it was a plan." Extensive — this is the central subject of the interview.
- **Confidence:** high (historiographical-thesis confidence, not Wikipedia-canonical confidence) — the parallel-planning thesis is speaker-originating but corroborated by the 2010 50th-anniversary retrospective and consistent with Bennett College's known institutional history. Note: any corpus entry should flag this as a historiographical-correction thesis, not a Wikipedia-canonical claim.
