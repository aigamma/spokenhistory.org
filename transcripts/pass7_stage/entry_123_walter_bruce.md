Review of the corrected transcript and the 140-entry corpus confirms the following figures and events from Entry 123 are **not yet in the corpus** and meet the threshold for addition:

### Proposal A: Robert G. Clark Jr.

**Name**: Robert G. Clark Jr.
**Role**: First Black to be elected to the Mississippi House of Representatives in the 20th century (November 1967, Holmes County, District 47). Served 36 years. Primary House sponsor of the 1982 Mississippi Education Reform Act.
**Why corpus**: Multiple oral-history entries in this corpus (including #107 and Entry 123) reference Clark's 1967 election as the canonical milestone of Holmes County Black electoral success. His election is directly supported by Bruce's first-person testimony ("Mr. Hayden, Robert Clark, and Dr. McLean") and confirmed as canonical across the Civil Rights History Project corpus. The corpus currently has Septima Clark but not Robert G. Clark Jr. — a significant gap given how many Holmes County entries cross-reference him.
**Transcript evidence**: "Rev. Eddie Clark, he was the first black that was, that became a candidate for US conference" (= Eddie Clark for Congress, distinct from Robert Clark for state legislature); "And that's when we came with Mr. Hayden, Robert Clark, and Dr. McLean" — Bruce's first-person co-architect testimony; Dittmer's setup confirms "Robert Clark became the first African American to sit in the Mississippi State Legislature in the 20th century."

### Proposal B: Alexander v. Holmes County Board of Education (1969)

**Name**: Alexander v. Holmes County Board of Education
**Role**: U.S. Supreme Court ruling (October 29, 1969, per curiam) that ordered immediate desegregation of Mississippi public schools, rejecting the "all deliberate speed" standard of Brown II (1955). The ruling directly affected Holmes County schools.
**Why corpus**: The corpus already has Brown v. Board of Education, Plessy v. Ferguson, and Loving v. Virginia. Alexander v. Holmes County (1969) is the direct sequel to Brown for Mississippi and is specifically named in this transcript by interviewer Dittmer: "the Holmes County decision in 1969, the US Supreme Court." Bruce then describes what happened locally when immediate integration was required. This is a foundational legal precedent for any Holmes County oral history corpus and is the only SCOTUS ruling that specifically names Holmes County in its title. Its absence from the 140-entry corpus is a significant gap.
**Transcript evidence**: "Once the Supreme Court decided in the Alexander v. Holmes County Board of Education, that the time for all the liberate speed was passed. And it was schools were going to have to be integrated immediately." (Dittmer setup; Bruce's response describes the local school-board response.)

### Proposal C: J.J. Young

**Name**: J.J. Young
**Role**: Canonical Durant-area Holmes County civil rights martyr; beaten and denied medical care (heart medication) while in the Lexington, Mississippi jail in the early 1960s; found dead the following morning. His death became the organizing impetus for Walter Bruce's Durant Movement boycott campaign.
**Why corpus**: Pass 2, Pass 3, and Pass 4 all flag J.J. Young as a ground-truth corpus candidate. Bruce's first-person testimony about Young's death is the most detailed first-person account in the Civil Rights History Project for this specific martyrdom. The account is internally consistent across the full transcript (name, location between Durant and West, locked up in Lexington, heart condition, medication refusal, morning discovery). Cross-corpus entries #107 and #111 may also reference Young. This is an important grassroots martyrdom that directly motivated Holmes County organizing and is not in the corpus.
**Transcript evidence**: "what happened to Jay Young, he lived between him, West, and some out, he went to the Lakson. They locked him up. Me like he was drunk or something like that. But then we knew that wasn't true... (J.J. Young) did have a bad heart. And I think that they refused to give him that medicine. And so that morning when they went in there, they found him dead."
